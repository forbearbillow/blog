# 你与你的研究 --- You and Your Research
https://www.cs.virginia.edu/~robins/YouAndYourResearch.html
Transcription of the  Bell Communications Research Colloquium Seminar

7 March 1986 

J. F. Kaiser

Bell Communications Research

445 South Street  Morristown, NJ 07962-1910

---

At a seminar in the Bell Communications Research Colloquia Series, Dr. Richard W. Hamming, a Professor at the Naval Postgraduate School in Monterey, California and a retired Bell Labs scientist, gave a very interesting and stimulating talk, \`You and Your Research' to an overflow audience of some 200 Bellcore staff members and visitors at the Morris Research and Engineering Center on March 7, 1986. This talk centered on Hamming's observations and research on the question \`\`Why do so few scientists make significant contributions and so many are forgotten in the long run?'' From his more than forty years of experience, thirty of which were at Bell Laboratories, he has made a number of direct observations, asked very pointed questions of scientists about what, how, and why they did things, studied the lives of great scientists and great contributions, and has done introspection and studied theories of creativity. The talk is about what he has learned in terms of the properties of the individual scientists, their abilities, traits, working habits, attitudes, and philosophy.

在贝尔通信研究研讨会系列的一次研讨会上，来自加利福尼亚州蒙蒂雷海军研究生院的教授、退休的贝尔实验室科学家理查德·W·哈明博士，于 1986 年 3 月 7 日在莫里斯研究与创新中心向约 200 名贝尔核心员工和访客发表了一场非常有趣且富有启发性的演讲，题目为《你与你的研究》。这次演讲围绕哈明博士对“为什么如此少的科学家做出重大贡献而许多人在长期内被遗忘”这一问题的观察和研究展开。凭借他四十多年的经验，其中三十年在贝尔实验室，他进行了许多直接的观察，向科学家们就他们做了什么、如何做以及为什么这样做提出了非常尖锐的问题，研究了伟大科学家和伟大贡献者的生平，并进行了自我反思，研究了创造力理论。这次演讲涉及他根据个体科学家的特性、能力、特质、工作习惯、态度和哲学所学到的东西。

In order to make the information in the talk more widely available, the tape recording that was made of that talk was carefully transcribed. This transcription includes the discussions which followed in the question and answer period. As with any talk, the transcribed version suffers from translation as all the inflections of voice and the gestures of the speaker are lost; one must listen to the tape recording to recapture that part of the presentation. While the recording of Richard Hamming's talk was completely intelligible, that of some of the questioner's remarks were not. Where the tape recording was not intelligible I have added in parentheses my impression of the questioner's remarks. Where there was a question and I could identify the questioner, I have checked with each to ensure the accuracy of my interpretation of their remarks.

为了使演讲中的信息更加广泛地传播，对那次演讲的录音进行了仔细的转录。这次转录包括了问答环节中的讨论。和任何演讲一样，转录版本由于失去了语音的语调和演讲者的手势而存在翻译问题；必须听录音才能重现演讲的这部分内容。虽然理查德·汉明演讲的录音完全清晰易懂，但有些提问者的发言并不清晰。在录音不清晰的地方，我在括号中添加了我对提问者发言的印象。在出现问题时，如果我能识别提问者，我会与每个人核对以确保我对他们发言的解释准确无误。

**INTRODUCTION OF DR. RICHARD W. HAMMING

理查德·W·汉明博士介绍**

As a speaker in the Bell Communications Research Colloquium Series, Dr. Richard W. Hamming of the Naval Postgraduate School in Monterey, California, was introduced by Alan G. Chynoweth, Vice President, Applied Research, Bell Communications Research.

作为贝尔通信研究研讨会系列的一名演讲者，来自加利福尼亚州蒙特雷的海军研究生院的理查德·W·哈明博士由贝尔通信研究应用研究副总裁艾伦·G·钦诺维茨介绍。

_Alan G. Chynoweth:_ Greetings colleagues, and also to many of our former colleagues from Bell Labs who, I understand, are here to be with us today on what I regard as a particularly felicitous occasion. It gives me very great pleasure indeed to introduce to you my old friend and colleague from many many years back, Richard Hamming, or Dick Hamming as he has always been know to all of us.

艾伦·G·钦诺维特：各位同事，以及许多从贝尔实验室来的前同事们，我了解到你们今天都在这里，共同度过一个我视为特别吉祥的时刻。我非常高兴地向大家介绍我的老朋友和多年的同事，理查德·汉明，或者像我们所有人一直称呼他的那样，迪克·汉明。

Dick is one of the all time greats in the mathematics and computer science arenas, as I'm sure the audience here does not need reminding. He received his early education at the Universities of Chicago and Nebraska, and got his Ph.D. at Illinois; he then joined the Los Alamos project during the war. Afterwards, in 1946, he joined Bell Labs. And that is, of course, where I met Dick - when I joined Bell Labs in their physics research organization. In those days, we were in the habit of lunching together as a physics group, and for some reason this strange fellow from mathematics was always pleased to join us. We were always happy to have him with us because he brought so many unorthodox ideas and views. Those lunches were stimulating, I can assure you.

迪克是数学和计算机科学领域中的杰出人物之一，这一点我相信在座的各位都无需我提醒。他早期在芝加哥大学和内布拉斯加大学接受教育，并在伊利诺伊大学获得博士学位；随后，他在二战期间加入了洛斯阿拉莫斯项目。之后，在 1946 年，他加入了贝尔实验室。当然，我就是在贝尔实验室加入他们的物理研究组织时认识迪克的。在那些日子里，我们习惯于作为一个物理小组一起吃午饭，不知出于什么原因，这位来自数学领域的奇特人物总是很高兴加入我们。我们总是很乐意有他陪伴，因为他带来了许多非传统的思想和观点。我向你保证，那些午饭时光是充满刺激的。

While our professional paths have not been very close over the years, nevertheless I've always recognized Dick in the halls of Bell Labs and have always had tremendous admiration for what he was doing. I think the record speaks for itself. It is too long to go through all the details, but let me point out, for example, that he has written seven books and of those seven books which tell of various areas of mathematics and computers and coding and information theory, three are already well into their second edition. That is testimony indeed to the prolific output and the stature of Dick Hamming.

虽然多年来我们的职业道路并没有很接近，但我一直都在贝尔实验室的大厅里认出迪克，并且一直对他所做的事情抱有极大的钦佩。我认为记录本身就说明了这一点。细节太多，无法一一列举，但让我举个例子，他写了七本书，其中关于数学、计算机、编码和信息理论的七本书中，有三本已经进入了它们的第二版。这确实证明了迪克·汉明的多产和地位。

I think I last met him - it must have been about ten years ago - at a rather curious little conference in Dublin, Ireland where we were both speakers. As always, he was tremendously entertaining. Just one more example of the provocative thoughts that he comes up with: I remember him saying, \`\`There are wavelengths that people cannot see, there are sounds that people cannot hear, and maybe computers have thoughts that people cannot think.'' Well, with Dick Hamming around, we don't need a computer. I think that we are in for an extremely entertaining talk.

我想我上次见到他——大概是在十年前——是在爱尔兰都柏林的一个相当奇特的小会议上，我们当时都是演讲者。一如既往，他非常有趣。他提出的挑衅性想法的一个例子：我记得他说，“有些波长是人眼看不见的，有些声音是人耳听不到的，也许计算机有人们想不到的思想。”好吧，有了迪克·汉明在身边，我们就不需要计算机了。我想我们将会听到一个极其有趣的谈话。

**THE TALK: \`\`You and Your Research'' by Dr. Richard W. Hamming

演讲：《你与你的研究》作者：理查德·W·哈明博士**

It's a pleasure to be here. I doubt if I can live up to the Introduction. The title of my talk is, \`\`You and Your Research.'' It is not about managing research, it is about how you individually do your research. I could give a talk on the other subject - but it's not, it's about you. I'm not talking about ordinary run-of-the-mill research; I'm talking about great research. And for the sake of describing great research I'll occasionally say Nobel-Prize type of work. It doesn't have to gain the Nobel Prize, but I mean those kinds of things which we perceive are significant things. Relativity, if you want, Shannon's information theory, any number of outstanding theories - that's the kind of thing I'm talking about.

很高兴来到这里。我怀疑自己能否达到引言的要求。我的演讲题目是《你与你的研究》。这并非关于管理研究，而是关于你如何个人进行研究。我可以就另一个主题发表演讲——但不是的，是关于你。我并非谈论普通的、常规的研究；我谈论的是伟大的研究。为了描述伟大的研究，我偶尔会提到诺贝尔奖级别的作品。它不一定获得诺贝尔奖，但指的是那些我们认为是重要事物的类型。相对论，如果你愿意的话，香农的信息论，无数杰出的理论——那就是我谈论的那种类型。

Now, how did I come to do this study? At Los Alamos I was brought in to run the computing machines which other people had got going, so those scientists and physicists could get back to business. I saw I was a stooge. I saw that although physically I was the same, they were different. And to put the thing bluntly, I was envious. I wanted to know why they were so different from me. I saw Feynman up close. I saw Fermi and Teller. I saw Oppenheimer. I saw Hans Bethe: he was my boss. I saw quite a few very capable people. I became very interested in the difference between those who do and those who might have done.

那么，我是如何进行这项研究的呢？在洛斯阿拉莫斯，我被请来管理其他人已经启动的计算机，这样那些科学家和物理学家就能重新投入工作。我意识到自己只是一个傀儡。我意识到，虽然我身体上和他们一样，但他们却不同。坦白地说，我感到嫉妒。我想知道他们为什么和我如此不同。我近距离观察了费曼。我观察了费米和泰勒。我观察了奥本海默。我观察了汉斯·贝特：他是我的老板。我观察了许多非常有能力的人。我对那些正在做事的人和那些本可以做事的人之间的差异产生了浓厚的兴趣。

When I came to Bell Labs, I came into a very productive department. Bode was the department head at the time; Shannon was there, and there were other people. I continued examining the questions, \`\`Why?'' and \`\`What is the difference?'' I continued subsequently by reading biographies, autobiographies, asking people questions such as: \`\`How did you come to do this?'' I tried to find out what are the differences. And that's what this talk is about.

当我来到贝尔实验室时，我加入了一个非常有生产力的部门。当时博德是部门负责人；香农在那里，还有其他一些人。我继续研究这些问题，“为什么？”和“有什么区别？”我随后通过阅读传记、自传，向人们提出这样的问题：“你是如何做到这一点的？”我试图找出有什么不同。这就是这次演讲的内容。

Now, why is this talk important? I think it is important because, as far as I know, each of you has one life to live. Even if you believe in reincarnation it doesn't do you any good from one life to the next! Why shouldn't you do significant things in this one life, however you define significant? I'm not going to define it - you know what I mean. I will talk mainly about science because that is what I have studied. But so far as I know, and I've been told by others, much of what I say applies to many fields. Outstanding work is characterized very much the same way in most fields, but I will confine myself to science.

现在，为什么这次演讲很重要？我认为它很重要，因为据我所知，你们每个人都只有一次生命可以生活。即使你相信轮回，它对你在这一生命到下一生命之间也没有任何好处！为什么你不应该在这一生命中做重要的事情，无论你如何定义重要？我不会定义它——你知道我的意思。我将主要谈论科学，因为这是我学习的东西。但据我所知，并且其他人告诉我，我所说的许多内容适用于许多领域。杰出的工作在大多数领域被非常相似地描述，但我将把自己局限于科学。

In order to get at you individually, I must talk in the first person. I have to get you to drop modesty and say to yourself, \`\`Yes, I would like to do first-class work.'' Our society frowns on people who set out to do really good work. You're not supposed to; luck is supposed to descend on you and you do great things by chance. Well, that's a kind of dumb thing to say. I say, why shouldn't you set out to do something significant. You don't have to tell other people, but shouldn't you say to yourself, \`\`Yes, I would like to do something significant.''

为了能单独与你交流，我必须使用第一人称。我必须让你放下谦虚，对自己说：“是的，我想做一流的工作。”我们的社会不赞成那些立志做真正出色工作的人。你不应该这样；幸运应该降临于你，你才能偶然做出伟大的事情。好吧，这种说法很愚蠢。我说，你为什么不能立志做一些有意义的事情。你不必告诉别人，但你难道不应该对自己说：“是的，我想做一些有意义的事情。”

In order to get to the second stage, I have to drop modesty and talk in the first person about what I've seen, what I've done, and what I've heard. I'm going to talk about people, some of whom you know, and I trust that when we leave, you won't quote me as saying some of the things I said.

为了进入第二阶段，我必须放下谦虚，用第一人称谈论我所见、所做、所闻。我将谈论一些人，其中一些你可能认识，我相信当我们离开时，你不会引用我说的一些话。

Let me start not logically, but psychologically. I find that the major objection is that people think great science is done by luck. It's all a matter of luck. Well, consider Einstein. Note how many different things he did that were good. Was it all luck? Wasn't it a little too repetitive? Consider Shannon. He didn't do just information theory. Several years before, he did some other good things and some which are still locked up in the security of cryptography. He did many good things.

让我从心理学的角度，而不是逻辑的角度开始。我发现主要的反对意见是人们认为伟大的科学是由运气完成的。这完全是运气。好吧，考虑一下爱因斯坦。注意他做了多少不同的事情是好的。这是全部都是运气吗？难道不是有点重复吗？考虑一下香农。他不仅做了信息论。几年前，他还做了一些其他的好事，一些仍然被密码学安全锁住的事情。他做了很多好事。

You see again and again, that it is more than one thing from a good person. Once in a while a person does only one thing in his whole life, and we'll talk about that later, but a lot of times there is repetition. I claim that luck will not cover everything. And I will cite Pasteur who said, \`\`Luck favors the prepared mind.'' And I think that says it the way I believe it. There is indeed an element of luck, and no, there isn't. The prepared mind sooner or later finds something important and does it. So yes, it is luck. The particular thing you do is luck, but that you do something is not.

你一次又一次地看到，一个优秀的人所做的事情远不止一件。有时候一个人一生只做一件事，我们后面会谈到这一点，但很多时候是重复的。我断言运气不会涵盖一切。我会引用巴斯德的话，他说：“运气偏爱有准备的头脑。”我认为这就是我信奉的方式。确实有运气成分，不，其实没有。有准备的头脑迟早会发现重要的事情并去做。所以是的，它是运气。你做的具体事情是运气，但你做某件事不是。

For example, when I came to Bell Labs, I shared an office for a while with Shannon. At the same time he was doing information theory, I was doing coding theory. It is suspicious that the two of us did it at the same place and at the same time - it was in the atmosphere. And you can say, \`\`Yes, it was luck.'' On the other hand you can say, \`\`But why of all the people in Bell Labs then were those the two who did it?'' Yes, it is partly luck, and partly it is the prepared mind; but \`partly' is the other thing I'm going to talk about. So, although I'll come back several more times to luck, I want to dispose of this matter of luck as being the sole criterion whether you do great work or not. I claim you have some, but not total, control over it. And I will quote, finally, Newton on the matter. Newton said, \`\`If others would think as hard as I did, then they would get similar results.''

例如，我刚到贝尔实验室时，曾和香农共用一间办公室一段时间。当时他研究信息论，我研究编码理论。我们俩在同一地点、同一时间做着同样的事情，这本身就很蹊跷——仿佛是某种巧合。你可能会说：“没错，这是运气。” 但另一方面，你也可能问：“为什么贝尔实验室那么多人，偏偏是他们俩做到了呢？” 是的，一部分是运气，一部分是头脑清醒；但“一部分”正是我要谈的另一点。所以，尽管我之后还会多次提到运气，但我希望先澄清一点：运气并非衡量你是否做出伟大工作的唯一标准。我认为，你对运气有一定的掌控力，但并非完全掌控。最后，我想引用牛顿关于此事的论述。牛顿说：“如果其他人像我一样认真思考，他们也会得到类似的结果。”

One of the characteristics you see, and many people have it including great scientists, is that usually when they were young they had independent thoughts and had the courage to pursue them. For example, Einstein, somewhere around 12 or 14, asked himself the question, \`\`What would a light wave look like if I went with the velocity of light to look at it?'' Now he knew that electromagnetic theory says you cannot have a stationary local maximum. But if he moved along with the velocity of light, he would see a local maximum. He could see a contradiction at the age of 12, 14, or somewhere around there, that everything was not right and that the velocity of light had something peculiar. Is it luck that he finally created special relativity? Early on, he had laid down some of the pieces by thinking of the fragments. Now that's the necessary but not sufficient condition. All of these items I will talk about are both luck and not luck.

你会发现，许多人，包括伟大的科学家，都具备一个共同的特质：他们**通常在年轻时就拥有独立思考的能力，并且有勇气去追求这些想法**。例如，爱因斯坦在12、14岁左右的时候就问自己：“如果我以光速去观察光波，它会是什么样子？”他当时知道电磁理论认为不可能存在静止的局部极大值。但如果他以光速运动，他就会看到一个局部极大值。他在12、14岁左右就发现了矛盾之处，意识到一切都不对劲，光速似乎有其特殊之处。他最终创立狭义相对论是偶然的吗？很早以前，他就通过思考一些零散的片段，为狭义相对论的创立奠定了基础。这是必要条件，但并非充分条件。我接下来要谈到的所有这些事情，既有偶然性，也有非偶然性。

How about having lots of \`brains?' It sounds good. Most of you in this room probably have more than enough brains to do first-class work. But great work is something else than mere brains. Brains are measured in various ways. In mathematics, theoretical physics, astrophysics, typically brains correlates to a great extent with the ability to manipulate symbols. And so the typical IQ test is apt to score them fairly high. On the other hand, in other fields it is something different. For example, Bill Pfann, the fellow who did zone melting, came into my office one day. He had this idea dimly in his mind about what he wanted and he had some equations. It was pretty clear to me that this man didn't know much mathematics and he wasn't really articulate. His problem seemed interesting so I took it home and did a little work. I finally showed him how to run computers so he could compute his own answers. I gave him the power to compute. He went ahead, with negligible recognition from his own department, but ultimately he has collected all the prizes in the field. Once he got well started, his shyness, his awkwardness, his inarticulateness, fell away and he became much more productive in many other ways. Certainly he became much more articulate.

怎么样拥有很多“智慧？” 听起来不错。在座的各位大多数可能拥有足够多的智慧来做一流的工作。但出色的工作不仅仅是智慧。智慧可以用多种方式来衡量。在数学、理论物理、天体物理中，智慧通常在很大程度上与操纵符号的能力相关。因此，典型的智商测试往往会给他们打很高的分。另一方面，在其他领域则有所不同。例如，做过区域熔炼的比尔·潘恩有一天来到我的办公室。他在脑海中模糊地有一个想法，知道自己想要什么，并且有一些方程。对我来说很清楚，这个人对数学了解不多，而且表达也不够清晰。他的问题似乎很有趣，所以我带回家做了一些工作。最后我教他如何使用计算机，这样他就可以自己计算答案。我给了他计算的能力。他继续前进，尽管他的部门几乎没有任何认可，但最终他获得了该领域的所有奖项。 一旦他开始顺利，他的羞怯、他的笨拙、他的不善于表达，都消失了，他在许多其他方面变得更有成效。当然，他变得善于表达多了。

And I can cite another person in the same way. I trust he isn't in the audience, i.e. a fellow named Clogston. I met him when I was working on a problem with John Pierce's group and I didn't think he had much. I asked my friends who had been with him at school, \`\`Was he like that in graduate school?'' \`\`Yes,'' they replied. Well I would have fired the fellow, but J. R. Pierce was smart and kept him on. Clogston finally did the Clogston cable. After that there was a steady stream of good ideas. One success brought him confidence and courage.

我还可以举另一个人的例子。我相信他不在现场，他叫克洛斯顿。我是在和约翰·皮尔斯的团队一起研究一个问题时认识他的，当时我觉得他没什么本事。我问了几个和他一起上过学的朋友：“他在研究生院的时候也这样吗？”他们回答说：“是的。”要是我，早就把他开除了，但J·R·皮尔斯很聪明，留了他。克洛斯顿最终发明了克洛斯顿电缆。从那以后，他源源不断地提出好点子。一次成功让他信心倍增，勇气倍增。

One of the characteristics of successful scientists is having courage. Once you get your courage up and believe that you can do important problems, then you can. If you think you can't, almost surely you are not going to. Courage is one of the things that Shannon had supremely. You have only to think of his major theorem. He wants to create a method of coding, but he doesn't know what to do so he makes a random code. Then he is stuck. And then he asks the impossible question, \`\`What would the average random code do?'' He then proves that the average code is arbitrarily good, and that therefore there must be at least one good code. Who but a man of infinite courage could have dared to think those thoughts? That is the characteristic of great scientists; they have courage. They will go forward under incredible circumstances; they think and continue to think.

成功科学家的一个特征是拥有勇气。**一旦你鼓起勇气并相信自己能够解决重要问题，那么你就能做到。如果你认为自己做不到，几乎肯定你将无法做到**。勇气是香农所拥有的至高无上的特质之一。你只需想想他的主要定理。他想创造一种编码方法，但他不知道该怎么做，于是他设计了一个随机编码。然后他陷入了困境。接着他提出了一个不可能的问题，“随机编码的平均效果会怎样？”然后他证明了平均编码是任意好的，因此必定存在至少一个好的编码。除了一个拥有无限勇气的人，还有谁敢想出这些想法？这就是伟大科学家的特征；他们拥有勇气。在难以置信的情况下，他们会勇往直前；他们会思考，并持续思考。

Age is another factor which the physicists particularly worry about. They always are saying that you have got to do it when you are young or you will never do it. Einstein did things very early, and all the quantum mechanic fellows were disgustingly young when they did their best work. Most mathematicians, theoretical physicists, and astrophysicists do what we consider their best work when they are young. It is not that they don't do good work in their old age but what we value most is often what they did early. On the other hand, in music, politics and literature, often what we consider their best work was done late. I don't know how whatever field you are in fits this scale, but age has some effect.

年龄是物理学家们特别关注的另一个因素。他们总是说，你**必须趁年轻去做，否则就永远做不成**。爱因斯坦很早就取得了成就，所有量子力学的先驱们在做出最杰出的工作时也都非常年轻。大多数数学家、理论物理学家和天体物理学家在年轻时都做出了我们认为最杰出的工作。这并不是说他们晚年就做不出好工作，而是我们最看重的往往是他们早期的成就。另一方面，在音乐、政治和文学领域，我们认为他们最杰出的作品往往是在晚年完成的。我不知道你所在的领域在这个尺度上处于什么位置，但年龄确实会产生一定的影响。

But let me say why age seems to have the effect it does. In the first place if you do some good work you will find yourself on all kinds of committees and unable to do any more work. You may find yourself as I saw Brattain when he got a Nobel Prize. The day the prize was announced we all assembled in Arnold Auditorium; all three winners got up and made speeches. The third one, Brattain, practically with tears in his eyes, said, \`\`I know about this Nobel-Prize effect and I am not going to let it affect me; I am going to remain good old Walter Brattain.'' Well I said to myself, \`\`That is nice.'' But in a few weeks I saw it was affecting him. Now he could only work on great problems.

但我想说说为什么年龄似乎会产生这样的影响。首先，如果你做了一些出色的工作，你会发现自己身兼数职，忙于各种委员会的工作，反而无法再做其他工作了。你可能会像我亲眼所见的布拉坦那样，在他获得诺贝尔奖之后。颁奖那天，我们都聚集在阿诺德礼堂；三位获奖者都起身发表了讲话。第三位获奖者布拉坦几乎热泪盈眶地说：“我知道诺贝尔奖效应，但我不会让它影响我；我还是那个好老沃尔特·布拉坦。” 我当时心想：“这真好。” 但几周后，我发现诺贝尔奖确实影响了他。现在他只能研究一些重大问题了。

When you are famous it is hard to work on small problems. This is what did Shannon in. After information theory, what do you do for an encore? The great scientists often make this error. They fail to continue to plant the little acorns from which the mighty oak trees grow. They try to get the big thing right off. And that isn't the way things go. So that is another reason why you find that when you get early recognition it seems to sterilize you. In fact I will give you my favorite quotation of many years. The Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton, in my opinion, has ruined more good scientists than any institution has created, judged by what they did before they came and judged by what they did after. Not that they weren't good afterwards, but they were superb before they got there and were only good afterwards.

当你成名之后，就很难专注于小问题。香农就是个例子。信息论之后，还能做什么呢？伟大的科学家们常常犯这个错误。他们未能继续播撒那些能长成参天大树的橡树籽。他们试图一举拿下最伟大的成就。但这并非事物发展的规律。所以，这也是为什么你会发现，过早获得认可似乎会扼杀你的创造力。事实上，我想分享一句我多年来最喜欢的名言：在我看来，普林斯顿高等研究院毁掉的优秀科学家比任何其他机构都多，无论从他们加入之前还是之后的成就来看都是如此。并非说他们之后就不优秀，而是说他们在加入之前就已经非常杰出，之后也只是平庸而已。

This brings up the subject, out of order perhaps, of working conditions. What most people think are the best working conditions, are not. Very clearly they are not because people are often most productive when working conditions are bad. One of the better times of the Cambridge Physical Laboratories was when they had practically shacks - they did some of the best physics ever.

这引出了一个话题，或许有点跑题，那就是工作条件。大多数人认为的最佳工作条件，其实并非如此。原因显而易见，因为人们往往在恶劣的工作条件下效率最高。剑桥物理实验室的鼎盛时期，恰恰是在简陋的棚屋里——他们却取得了有史以来最杰出的物理成就。

I give you a story from my own private life. Early on it became evident to me that Bell Laboratories was not going to give me the conventional acre of programming people to program computing machines in absolute binary. It was clear they weren't going to. But that was the way everybody did it. I could go to the West Coast and get a job with the airplane companies without any trouble, but the exciting people were at Bell Labs and the fellows out there in the airplane companies were not. I thought for a long while about, \`\`Did I want to go or not?'' and I wondered how I could get the best of two possible worlds. I finally said to myself, \`\`Hamming, you think the machines can do practically everything. Why can't you make them write programs?'' What appeared at first to me as a defect forced me into automatic programming very early. What appears to be a fault, often, by a change of viewpoint, turns out to be one of the greatest assets you can have. But you are not likely to think that when you first look the thing and say, \`\`Gee, I'm never going to get enough programmers, so how can I ever do any great programming?''

我给你讲一个我亲身经历的故事。很早我就意识，贝尔实验室不会给我提供传统的那种编程人员来用绝对二进制编程计算机。他们显然不会这么做。但当时大家都是这么做的。我可以毫不费力地去西海岸的飞机公司找份工作，但真正有才华的人都在贝尔实验室，而飞机公司里的人却不是。我纠结了很久：“我到底要不要去？”我一直在想，怎样才能两全其美。最后我对自己说：“汉明，你认为机器几乎无所不能。为什么不能让它们自己编写程序呢？”起初在我看来是个缺陷，但正是这个缺陷迫使我很早就开始学习自动编程。**很多时候，看似缺点的东西，换个角度看，反而会成为你最大的优势之一**。但你第一眼看到这个问题时，不太可能会想：“哎呀，我永远也找不到足够的程序员，那我怎么可能做出伟大的编程呢？”

And there are many other stories of the same kind; Grace Hopper has similar ones. I think that if you look carefully you will see that often the great scientists, by turning the problem around a bit, changed a defect to an asset. For example, many scientists when they found they couldn't do a problem finally began to study why not. They then turned it around the other way and said, \`\`But of course, this is what it is'' and got an important result. So ideal working conditions are very strange. The ones you want aren't always the best ones for you.

还有许多类似的故事；格蕾丝·霍珀也有类似的经历。我认为如果你仔细观察，你会发现许多伟大的科学家通过稍微转变一下问题，将缺陷变成了优势。例如，许多科学家在发现他们无法解决一个问题时，开始研究为什么不行。然后他们反过来想，说，“当然，这就是它的本质”，并得到了一个重要的结果。所以，理想的工作条件其实很奇特。你想要的并不总是最适合你的。

Now for the matter of drive. You observe that most great scientists have tremendous drive. I worked for ten years with John Tukey at Bell Labs. He had tremendous drive. One day about three or four years after I joined, I discovered that John Tukey was slightly younger than I was. John was a genius and I clearly was not. Well I went storming into Bode's office and said, \`\`How can anybody my age know as much as John Tukey does?'' He leaned back in his chair, put his hands behind his head, grinned slightly, and said, \`\`You would be surprised Hamming, how much you would know if you worked as hard as he did that many years.'' I simply slunk out of the office!

现在谈谈动力的问题。你观察到**大多数伟大的科学家都有极强的动力**。我在贝尔实验室与约翰·图基共事十年。他有着极强的动力。在我加入后大约三四年，我发现约翰·图基比我稍年轻一些。约翰是个天才，而我很清楚我不是。于是我怒气冲冲地冲进博德的办公室，说：“像我这么大年纪的人怎么能知道约翰·图基那么多的东西？”他往后一靠椅子，双手放在头后，微微一笑，说：“哈明，你会惊讶的，如果你像他那样努力工作这么多年，你会知道多少。”我只好灰溜溜地走出办公室！

What Bode was saying was this: \`\`Knowledge and productivity are like compound interest.'' Given two people of approximately the same ability and one person who works ten percent more than the other, the latter will more than twice outproduce the former. The more you know, the more you learn; the more you learn, the more you can do; the more you can do, the more the opportunity - it is very much like compound interest. I don't want to give you a rate, but it is a very high rate. Given two people with exactly the same ability, the one person who manages day in and day out to get in one more hour of thinking will be tremendously more productive over a lifetime. I took Bode's remark to heart; I spent a good deal more of my time for some years trying to work a bit harder and I found, in fact, I could get more work done. I don't like to say it in front of my wife, but I did sort of neglect her sometimes; I needed to study. You have to neglect things if you intend to get what you want done. There's no question about this.

博德所说的意思是这样的：“**知识和生产力就像复利**。”假设两个人能力相近，其中一个人比另一个人多工作 10%，那么后者一生中的产出将超过前者两倍以上。你懂得越多，学到的东西就越多；学到的东西越多，能做的事情就越多；能做的事情越多，机会就越多——这和复利非常相似。我不想给出具体的利率，但这是一个非常高的利率。假设两个人能力完全相同，那个每天努力多思考一个小时的人，一生中的生产力将大大提高。我把博德的话牢记在心；有好几年，我投入了更多的时间努力工作，结果发现，我确实能完成更多的工作。我不太愿意当着妻子的面说，但我有时确实会忽略她；因为我需要学习。如果你想达成目标，就必须有所取舍。这一点毋庸置疑。

On this matter of drive Edison says, \`\`Genius is 99% perspiration and 1% inspiration.'' He may have been exaggerating, but the idea is that solid work, steadily applied, gets you surprisingly far. The steady application of effort with a little bit more work, intelligently applied is what does it. That's the trouble; drive, misapplied, doesn't get you anywhere. I've often wondered why so many of my good friends at Bell Labs who worked as hard or harder than I did, didn't have so much to show for it. The misapplication of effort is a very serious matter. Just hard work is not enough - it must be applied sensibly.

关于动力，爱迪生曾说过：“天才 99%靠汗水，1%靠灵感。”他或许有些夸张，但其核心思想是：扎实的工作，持之以恒，能让你取得意想不到的成就。而真正奏效的，是持之以恒的努力， 再加上一些更明智的努力。问题就在这里：动力如果用错了地方，就一事无成。我常常纳闷，为什么我在贝尔实验室的那么多好朋友，他们和我一样努力，甚至比我更努力，却没有取得多少成就。努力用错了地方，这可不是一件小事。光靠努力是不够的——必须懂得如何明智地运用它。

There's another trait on the side which I want to talk about; that trait is ambiguity. It took me a while to discover its importance. Most people like to believe something is or is not true. Great scientists tolerate ambiguity very well. They believe the theory enough to go ahead; they doubt it enough to notice the errors and faults so they can step forward and create the new replacement theory. If you believe too much you'll never notice the flaws; if you doubt too much you won't get started. It requires a lovely balance. But most great scientists are well aware of why their theories are true and they are also well aware of some slight misfits which don't quite fit and they don't forget it. Darwin writes in his autobiography that he found it necessary to write down every piece of evidence which appeared to contradict his beliefs because otherwise they would disappear from his mind. When you find apparent flaws you've got to be sensitive and keep track of those things, and keep an eye out for how they can be explained or how the theory can be changed to fit them. Those are often the great contributions. Great contributions are rarely done by adding another decimal place. It comes down to an emotional commitment. Most great scientists are completely committed to their problem. Those who don't become committed seldom produce outstanding, first-class work.

我想谈谈另一个方面，那就是模糊性。我花了很长时间才意识到它的重要性。**大多数人喜欢相信某件事是真或不是真。伟大的科学家却能很好地容忍模糊性**。他们对理论的信念足以让他们继续前进；他们也对理论抱有足够的怀疑，以便发现其中的错误和缺陷，从而提出新的替代理论。如果你过于相信，就永远不会注意到缺陷；如果你过于怀疑，就无法开始。这需要一种微妙的平衡。但大多数伟大的科学家都清楚地知道他们的理论为何正确，他们也清楚地知道一些不太吻合的细微偏差，并且他们不会忘记这些偏差。达尔文在他的自传中写道，他发现**有必要记录下所有看似与他的信念相矛盾的证据，否则这些证据就会从他的脑海中消失。当你发现明显的缺陷时，你必须保持敏感，记录下这些内容，并留意如何解释它们，或者如何修改理论以使其符合这些缺陷。这些往往是伟大的贡献**。伟大的贡献很少是通过增加一个小数位来实现的，关键在于情感上的投入。大多数伟大的科学家都全身心地投入到他们研究的课题中。那些没有全身心投入的人，很少能做出杰出的一流成果。

Now again, emotional commitment is not enough. It is a necessary condition apparently. And I think I can tell you the reason why. Everybody who has studied creativity is driven finally to saying, \`\`creativity comes out of your subconscious.'' Somehow, suddenly, there it is. It just appears. Well, we know very little about the subconscious; but one thing you are pretty well aware of is that your dreams also come out of your subconscious. And you're aware your dreams are, to a fair extent, a reworking of the experiences of the day. If you are deeply immersed and committed to a topic, day after day after day, your subconscious has nothing to do but work on your problem. And so you wake up one morning, or on some afternoon, and there's the answer. For those who don't get committed to their current problem, the subconscious goofs off on other things and doesn't produce the big result. So the way to manage yourself is that when you have a real important problem you don't let anything else get the center of your attention - you keep your thoughts on the problem. Keep your subconscious starved so it has to work on your problem, so you can sleep peacefully and get the answer in the morning, free.

然而，仅仅有情感投入是不够的。这显然是一个必要条件。我想我可以告诉你其中的原因。所有研究过创造力的人最终都会得出这样的结论：“**创造力源于你的潜意识**。”不知何故，它突然就出现了。它就这么凭空出现了。我们对潜意识知之甚少；但有一点你很清楚，那就是你的梦境也源于潜意识。你也知道，在很大程度上，你的梦境是对白天经历的重新加工。如果你日复一日地全身心投入到某个主题中，你的潜意识就会专注于你的问题。所以，你某天早上醒来，或者某个下午，答案就出现了。对于那些没有全身心投入到当前问题中的人来说，潜意识会去忙其他事情，而不会产生重要的结果。因此，管理自己的方法是，**当你遇到一个真正重要的问题时，不要让任何其他事情分散你的注意力——你要始终专注于这个问题。让你的潜意识保持饥饿状态，这样它就不得不去思考你的问题**，这样你就可以安心睡觉，并在早上免费获得答案。

Now Alan Chynoweth mentioned that I used to eat at the physics table. I had been eating with the mathematicians and I found out that I already knew a fair amount of mathematics; in fact, I wasn't learning much. The physics table was, as he said, an exciting place, but I think he exaggerated on how much I contributed. It was very interesting to listen to Shockley, Brattain, Bardeen, J. B. Johnson, Ken McKay and other people, and I was learning a lot. But unfortunately a Nobel Prize came, and a promotion came, and what was left was the dregs. Nobody wanted what was left. Well, there was no use eating with them!

现在艾伦·钦诺威特提到我以前常在物理桌吃饭。我和数学家们一起吃饭，我发现我已经知道了很多数学；事实上，我并没有学到太多。正如他所说，物理桌是一个令人兴奋的地方，但我认为他夸大了我的贡献。听休克雷、布拉顿、巴丁、J. B. 约翰逊、肯·麦凯和其他人的谈话非常有趣，我学到了很多东西。但不幸的是，诺贝尔奖来了，升职了，剩下的都是些残羹剩饭。没有人想要剩下的东西。唉，和它们一起吃饭是没有用的！

Over on the other side of the dining hall was a chemistry table. I had worked with one of the fellows, Dave McCall; furthermore he was courting our secretary at the time. I went over and said, \`\`Do you mind if I join you?'' They can't say no, so I started eating with them for a while. And I started asking, \`\`What are the important problems of your field?'' And after a week or so, \`\`What important problems are you working on?'' And after some more time I came in one day and said, \`\`If what you are doing is not important, and if you don't think it is going to lead to something important, why are you at Bell Labs working on it?'' I wasn't welcomed after that; I had to find somebody else to eat with! That was in the spring.

餐厅另一边是化学系的桌子。我之前和其中一位同事戴夫·麦考尔共事过；而且当时他还在追求我们的秘书。我走过去问：“我可以和你们一起吃吗？”他们不好拒绝，所以我就和他们一起吃了一段时间。之后我开始问：“**你们领域里有哪些重要的问题？**”大约一周后，我又问：“**你们正在研究哪些重要的问题？**”又过了一段时间，有一天我走进去，说：“**如果你们的研究不重要，如果你们认为它不会带来任何重要的成果，那你们为什么还要在贝尔实验室做这个？**”之后我就不受欢迎了；我只好另找人一起吃饭！那是春天的时候。

In the fall, Dave McCall stopped me in the hall and said, \`\`Hamming, that remark of yours got underneath my skin. I thought about it all summer, i.e. what were the important problems in my field. I haven't changed my research,'' he says, \`\`but I think it was well worthwhile.'' And I said, \`\`Thank you Dave,'' and went on. I noticed a couple of months later he was made the head of the department. I noticed the other day he was a Member of the National Academy of Engineering. I noticed he has succeeded. I have never heard the names of any of the other fellows at that table mentioned in science and scientific circles. They were unable to ask themselves, \`\`What are the important problems in my field?''

秋天的时候，戴夫·麦考尔在走廊里拦住我，说：“汉明，你那句话让我耿耿于怀。整个夏天我都在思考这个问题，也就是我研究领域里真正重要的问题是什么。”他说，“我的研究方向没有改变，但我认为那次思考非常有价值。” 我说：“谢谢你，戴夫，”然后继续做我的事。几个月后，我注意到他被任命为系主任。前几天我又注意到他当选为美国国家工程院院士。我注意到他成功了。我从未在科学界或科学界听到过那桌其他任何人的名字。他们根本无法问自己：“我研究领域里真正重要的问题是什么？”

If you do not work on an important problem, it's unlikely you'll do important work. It's perfectly obvious. Great scientists have thought through, in a careful way, a number of important problems in their field, and they keep an eye on wondering how to attack them. Let me warn you, \`important problem' must be phrased carefully. The three outstanding problems in physics, in a certain sense, were never worked on while I was at Bell Labs. By important I mean guaranteed a Nobel Prize and any sum of money you want to mention. We didn't work on (1) time travel, (2) teleportation, and (3) antigravity. They are not important problems because we do not have an attack. It's not the consequence that makes a problem important, it is that you have a reasonable attack. That is what makes a problem important. When I say that most scientists don't work on important problems, I mean it in that sense. The average scientist, so far as I can make out, spends almost all his time working on problems which he believes will not be important and he also doesn't believe that they will lead to important problems.

**如果你不去研究一个重要的问题，你就不太可能做出重要的工作**。这显而易见。伟大的科学家们都曾认真思考过他们领域内的许多重要问题，并且他们始终关注着如何解决这些问题。但我要提醒你，“重要问题”这个概念必须谨慎使用。从某种意义上说，物理学中三个悬而未决的问题，在我待在贝尔实验室期间，从未有人着手研究。我所说的“重要”，是指那些能够确保获得诺贝尔奖以及任何你想要的奖金的问题。我们没有研究过（1）时间旅行、（2）瞬间移动和（3）反重力。它们之所以不是重要的问题，是因为我们还没有找到解决办法。**使一个问题变得重要的，不是其结果，而是你是否找到了合理的解决办法**。这才是问题重要性的所在。当我说大多数科学家没有研究重要的问题时，我的意思正是如此。据我所知，普通科学家几乎把所有时间都花在研究他认为不重要，也不认为会导致重要问题的问题上。

I spoke earlier about planting acorns so that oaks will grow. You can't always know exactly where to be, but you can keep active in places where something might happen. And even if you believe that great science is a matter of luck, you can stand on a mountain top where lightning strikes; you don't have to hide in the valley where you're safe. But the average scientist does routine safe work almost all the time and so he (or she) doesn't produce much. It's that simple. If you want to do great work, you clearly must work on important problems, and you should have an idea.

我之前说过，要种橡子才能长出橡树。你不可能总是确切地知道自己该在哪里，但你可以积极地出现在可能发生事情的地方。即使你认为伟大的科学成就取决于运气，你也可以站在雷电交加的山顶上；你不必躲在安全的山谷里。但普通科学家几乎总是做着例行公事般的安全工作，所以他们（或她）产出不多。就这么简单。**如果你想做出伟大的成就，你显然必须致力于解决重要的问题，而且你必须要有想法。**

Along those lines at some urging from John Tukey and others, I finally adopted what I called \`\`Great Thoughts Time.'' When I went to lunch Friday noon, I would only discuss great thoughts after that. By great thoughts I mean ones like: \`\`What will be the role of computers in all of AT&T?'', \`\`How will computers change science?'' For example, I came up with the observation at that time that nine out of ten experiments were done in the lab and one in ten on the computer. I made a remark to the vice presidents one time, that it would be reversed, i.e. nine out of ten experiments would be done on the computer and one in ten in the lab. They knew I was a crazy mathematician and had no sense of reality. I knew they were wrong and they've been proved wrong while I have been proved right. They built laboratories when they didn't need them. I saw that computers were transforming science because I spent a lot of time asking \`\`What will be the impact of computers on science and how can I change it?'' I asked myself, \`\`How is it going to change Bell Labs?'' I remarked one time, in the same address, that more than one-half of the people at Bell Labs will be interacting closely with computing machines before I leave. Well, you all have terminals now. I thought hard about where was my field going, where were the opportunities, and what were the important things to do. Let me go there so there is a chance I can do important things.

在约翰·图基等人的敦促下，我最终采用了我所谓的“伟大思考时间”。每周五中午吃午饭后，我只讨论伟大的想法。我所说的伟大想法指的是诸如“计算机在 AT&T 的整体运营中将扮演什么角色？”、“计算机将如何改变科学？”之类的问题。例如，我当时观察到，十个实验中有九个是在实验室完成的，只有一个是在计算机上完成的。我曾经跟副总裁们说过，情况会反过来，也就是说，十个实验中有九个会在计算机上完成，只有一个会在实验室完成。他们知道我是一个疯狂的数学家，完全脱离现实。我知道他们错了，事实证明他们错了，而我是对的。他们建造实验室的时候，其实并不需要。我意识到计算机正在改变科学，是因为我花了大量时间思考“计算机将对科学产生什么影响？我又该如何改变它？”我问自己，“它将如何改变贝尔实验室？”我曾在一次演讲中提到，在我离开之前，贝尔实验室超过一半的人都将与计算机密切接触。现在，你们都有终端了。我认真思考了我的领域将走向何方，机遇在哪里，以及应该做些什么重要的事情。让我去那里，这样我才有机会做些重要的事情。”

Most great scientists know many important problems. They have something between 10 and 20 important problems for which they are looking for an attack. And when they see a new idea come up, one hears them say \`\`Well that bears on this problem.'' They drop all the other things and get after it. Now I can tell you a horror story that was told to me but I can't vouch for the truth of it. I was sitting in an airport talking to a friend of mine from Los Alamos about how it was lucky that the fission experiment occurred over in Europe when it did because that got us working on the atomic bomb here in the US. He said \`\`No; at Berkeley we had gathered a bunch of data; we didn't get around to reducing it because we were building some more equipment, but if we had reduced that data we would have found fission.'' They had it in their hands and they didn't pursue it. They came in second!

大多数伟大的科学家都知道许多重要的问题。他们通常有 10 到 20个重要的问题需要解决。当他们看到一个新想法出现时，人们会听到他们说：“嗯，这跟这个问题有关。”然后他们会放下其他所有的事情，全力以赴。现在我可以告诉你一个恐怖故事，这个故事是别人告诉我的，但我不能保证它的真实性。当时我坐在机场，和一位来自洛斯阿拉莫斯的朋友聊天，我们聊到，裂变实验恰好率先在欧洲开展实属万幸，正是这件事推动美国本土开启了原子弹的研发工作。他却说：“并非如此。我们伯克利团队当时已经收集了大量相关数据，只是因为一直在加装、制造更多实验设备，迟迟没有来得及整理分析这些数据。倘若我们当时完成了数据处理，就一定能率先发现核裂变现象。”关键的研究线索本就掌握在他们手中，却没能继续深入探索，最终遗憾错失先机，屈居人后。

The great scientists, when an opportunity opens up, get after it and they pursue it. They drop all other things. They get rid of other things and they get after an idea because they had already thought the thing through. Their minds are prepared; they see the opportunity and they go after it. Now of course lots of times it doesn't work out, but you don't have to hit many of them to do some great science. It's kind of easy. One of the chief tricks is to live a long time!

**伟大的科学家一旦遇到机会，就会全力以赴，毫不犹豫地抓住它。他们会放下其他一切，摒弃其他事务，一心扑在某个想法上，因为他们早已深思熟虑**。他们的头脑早已做好准备，一旦发现机会，便会立即行动。当然，很多时候，机会并不总能如愿以偿，但你并不需要抓住很多机会才能做出伟大的科学成就。这其实很简单。其中一个关键秘诀就是：活得长久！

Another trait, it took me a while to notice. I noticed the following facts about people who work with the door open or the door closed. I notice that if you have the door to your office closed, you get more work done today and tomorrow, and you are more productive than most. But 10 years later somehow you don't know quite know what problems are worth working on; all the hard work you do is sort of tangential in importance. He who works with the door open gets all kinds of interruptions, but he also occasionally gets clues as to what the world is and what might be important. Now I cannot prove the cause and effect sequence because you might say, \`\`The closed door is symbolic of a closed mind.'' I don't know. But I can say there is a pretty good correlation between those who work with the doors open and those who ultimately do important things, although people who work with doors closed often work harder. Somehow they seem to work on slightly the wrong thing - not much, but enough that they miss fame.

另一个特点，我花了一段时间才注意到。我注意到，有些人工作时开着门，有些人则关着门。我发现，**如果你关着办公室的门，你今天和明天的工作效率会更高，比大多数人都更高效。但十年后，你却不知为何不太清楚哪些问题值得解决**；你所做的所有努力似乎都无关紧要。开着门工作的人会受到各种各样的干扰，但偶尔也能从中获得一些关于世界本质和重要事物的线索。我无法证明这种因果关系，因为你可能会说，“关着门象征着封闭的思想。”我不知道。但我可以肯定的是，开着门工作的人和最终成就大事的人之间存在着相当强的相关性，尽管关着门工作的人往往更加努力。不知何故，他们似乎总是在做一些略微错误的事情——虽然不多，但足以让他们与名声失之交臂。

I want to talk on another topic. It is based on the song which I think many of you know, \`\`It ain't what you do, it's the way that you do it.'' I'll start with an example of my own. I was conned into doing on a digital computer, in the absolute binary days, a problem which the best analog computers couldn't do. And I was getting an answer. When I thought carefully and said to myself, \`\`You know, Hamming, you're going to have to file a report on this military job; after you spend a lot of money you're going to have to account for it and every analog installation is going to want the report to see if they can't find flaws in it.'' I was doing the required integration by a rather crummy method, to say the least, but I was getting the answer. And I realized that in truth the problem was not just to get the answer; it was to demonstrate for the first time, and beyond question, that I could beat the analog computer on its own ground with a digital machine. I reworked the method of solution, created a theory which was nice and elegant, and changed the way we computed the answer; the results were no different. The published report had an elegant method which was later known for years as \`\`Hamming's Method of Integrating Differential Equations.'' It is somewhat obsolete now, but for a while it was a very good method. By changing the problem slightly, I did important work rather than trivial work.

我想谈谈另一个话题。这个话题源于一首歌，我想你们很多人都听过：“重要的不是你做了什么，而是你怎么做。” 我先举个我自己的例子。在绝对二进制的时代，我被骗用一台数字计算机解决了一个当时最好的模拟计算机都无法解决的问题。而且我居然得到了答案。后来我仔细想了想，对自己说：“你知道吗，汉明，你得就这个军事项目提交一份报告；你花了那么多钱，就得解释清楚，而且每个模拟计算机都会想要这份报告，看看能不能找出其中的漏洞。” 说实话，我当时用的方法相当糟糕，但我得到了答案。我意识到，真正的问题不仅仅是得到答案；而是要第一次、毫无疑问地证明，我可以用一台数字计算机在模拟计算机擅长的领域里战胜它。我改进了解题方法，建立了一个简洁优雅的理论，并改变了计算结果的方式；结果并无不同。发表的报告采用了一种精妙的方法，后来多年来一直被称为“汉明微分方程积分法”。现在它已经有些过时了，但曾经是一个非常有效的方法。通过对问题稍作修改，我完成了重要的工作，而不是琐碎的工作。

In the same way, when using the machine up in the attic in the early days, I was solving one problem after another after another; a fair number were successful and there were a few failures. I went home one Friday after finishing a problem, and curiously enough I wasn't happy; I was depressed. I could see life being a long sequence of one problem after another after another. After quite a while of thinking I decided, \`\`No, I should be in the mass production of a variable product. I should be concerned with _all_ of next year's problems, not just the one in front of my face.'' By changing the question I still got the same kind of results or better, but I changed things and did important work. I attacked the major problem - How do I conquer machines and do all of next year's problems when I don't know what they are going to be? How do I prepare for it? How do I do this one so I'll be on top of it? How do I obey Newton's rule? He said, \`\`If I have seen further than others, it is because I've stood on the shoulders of giants.'' These days we stand on each other's feet!

同样地，早年我在阁楼使用那台机器时，总是接连不断地解决一个又一个问题；其中不少问题顺利攻克，也有少数以失败告终。某个周五，我解决完一个问题后回到家中，奇怪的是，我毫无喜悦之感，反而满心低落。我忽然意识到，人生本就是一连串接踵而至的难题。深思许久后，我下定决心：“不，我应当投身于多元化产品的规模化研发。我要着眼于明年所有的难题，而非只纠结于眼前这一个。”通过转换思考的角度，我不仅收获了同等甚至更优的成果，还调整了做事方式，完成了诸多关键性工作。我开始直面核心难题：在无从预知明年各类问题的前提下，该如何驾驭机器、提前应对所有未知挑战？该如何做好万全准备？该如何做好当下之事，从而牢牢掌握主动权？又该如何践行牛顿的理念？牛顿曾说：“如果我看得比别人更远，那是因为我站在巨人的肩膀上。”而如今，我们却常常相互掣肘、彼此拖累。

You should do your job in such a fashion that others can build on top of it, so they will indeed say, \`\`Yes, I've stood on so and so's shoulders and I saw further.'' The essence of science is cumulative. By changing a problem slightly you can often do great work rather than merely good work. Instead of attacking isolated problems, I made the resolution that I would never again solve an isolated problem except as characteristic of a class.

你应该以这样的方式完成你的工作：让其他人能够在此基础上继续发展，这样他们才能真正地说：“是的，我站在某某的肩膀上，看得更远。”科学的本质是累积性的。通过对问题稍作调整，你往往可以做出伟大的工作，而不仅仅是优秀的工作。我不再孤立地看待问题，而是下定决心，除非问题具有某一类特有的特征，否则我绝不再解决孤立的问题。

Now if you are much of a mathematician you know that the effort to generalize often means that the solution is simple. Often by stopping and saying, \`\`This is the problem he wants but this is characteristic of so and so. Yes, I can attack the whole class with a far superior method than the particular one because I was earlier embedded in needless detail.'' The business of abstraction frequently makes things simple. Furthermore, I filed away the methods and prepared for the future problems.

倘若你具备一定的数学素养，就会明白，**尝试进行归纳推广，往往能让解法变得简洁**。很多时候，我们不妨停下来思考：“这只是具体问题的诉求，但其背后蕴含着某类事物的共性特征。没错，我可以用远比特殊解法更优的方式，攻克整一类问题，只因我此前深陷在无谓的细节之中。”抽象化思维，往往能化繁为简。除此之外，我还会将各类解题方法整理归纳，为日后遇到的难题做好铺垫。

To end this part, I'll remind you, \`\`It is a poor workman who blames his tools - the good man gets on with the job, given what he's got, and gets the best answer he can.'' And I suggest that by altering the problem, by looking at the thing differently, you can make a great deal of difference in your final productivity because you can either do it in such a fashion that people can indeed build on what you've done, or you can do it in such a fashion that the next person has to essentially duplicate again what you've done. It isn't just a matter of the job, it's the way you write the report, the way you write the paper, the whole attitude. It's just as easy to do a broad, general job as one very special case. And it's much more satisfying and rewarding!

最后，我想提醒大家一句：“拙匠只会抱怨工具不好，而好匠则会利用手头现有的工具，尽力做到最好。” 我认为，通过改变问题的角度，换个角度看待问题，就能显著提高最终的效率。你可以选择一种方式，**让其他人能够在此基础上继续发展**；也可以选择一种方式，让下一个人不得不重复你之前的工作。这不仅仅关乎工作本身，还关乎你撰写报告的方式、你撰写论文的方式，以及你整体的态度。处理一个宽泛的、通用性的工作和处理一个非常特殊的案例一样容易，而且前者更有成就感，也更有成就感！

I have now come down to a topic which is very distasteful; it is not sufficient to do a job, you have to sell it. \`Selling' to a scientist is an awkward thing to do. It's very ugly; you shouldn't have to do it. The world is supposed to be waiting, and when you do something great, they should rush out and welcome it. But the fact is everyone is busy with their own work. You must present it so well that they will set aside what they are doing, look at what you've done, read it, and come back and say, \`\`Yes, that was good.'' I suggest that when you open a journal, as you turn the pages, you ask why you read some articles and not others. You had better write your report so when it is published in the Physical Review, or wherever else you want it, as the readers are turning the pages they won't just turn your pages but they will stop and read yours. If they don't stop and read it, you won't get credit.

现在我要谈的是一个令人非常不快的话题：仅仅完成一项工作是不够的，你还得把它推销出去。“推销”给科学家是一件很尴尬的事，非常难堪，你本不应该被迫去做。人们理应翘首以盼，当你做出伟大的成就时，他们应该蜂拥而至，欣然接受。但事实是，每个人都忙于自己的工作。**你必须把你的工作展现得淋漓尽致，让他们放下手头的工作，认真阅读你的成果**，然后回来赞叹：“没错，写得真好。” 我建议你翻阅期刊时，**问问自己为什么有些文章你会读，而有些却不会**。你最好把你的报告写好，这样当它发表在《物理评论》或其他任何你想发表的地方时，读者在翻阅期刊时，不仅会翻到你的部分，还会停下来仔细阅读你的文章。如果他们没有停下来阅读，你就得不到任何认可。

There are three things you have to do in selling. You have to learn to write clearly and well so that people will read it, you must learn to give reasonably formal talks, and you also must learn to give informal talks. We had a lot of so-called \`back room scientists.' In a conference, they would keep quiet. Three weeks later after a decision was made they filed a report saying why you should do so and so. Well, it was too late. They would not stand up right in the middle of a hot conference, in the middle of activity, and say, \`\`We should do this for these reasons.'' You need to master that form of communication as well as prepared speeches.

在销售中，有三个方面你必须做到。**首先，你要学会清晰流畅地写作，让别人愿意阅读；其次，你要学会进行较为正式的演讲；最后，你还要学会进行非正式的交流**。我们有很多所谓的“幕后科学家”。在会议上，他们会保持沉默。三周后，当决策做出时，他们提交了一份报告，说明为什么应该这样做。但是，那时已经太晚了。他们不会在热烈的会议中，在活动中间，站起来说：“我们应该为此原因这样做。” 你也需要掌握这种沟通形式，以及事先准备好的演讲稿。

When I first started, I got practically physically ill while giving a speech, and I was very, very nervous. I realized I either had to learn to give speeches smoothly or I would essentially partially cripple my whole career. The first time IBM asked me to give a speech in New York one evening, I decided I was going to give a really good speech, a speech that was wanted, not a technical one but a broad one, and at the end if they liked it, I'd quietly say, \`\`Any time you want one I'll come in and give you one.'' As a result, I got a great deal of practice giving speeches to a limited audience and I got over being afraid. Furthermore, I could also then study what methods were effective and what were ineffective.

我刚开始演讲的时候，每次演讲都紧张得几乎要吐出来，我当时非常非常紧张。我意识到，要么我必须学会流畅地演讲，要么我的整个职业生涯都会因此而大打折扣。IBM 第一次邀请我去纽约演讲那天晚上，我决定一定要做一场精彩的演讲，一场他们真正想听的演讲，不是那种技术性的，而是内容广泛的。演讲结束后，如果他们满意，我会悄悄地说：“以后你们需要演讲的时候，我随时可以来。” 结果，我积累了大量面向有限听众演讲的经验，也克服了恐惧。此外，我还可以借此机会研究哪些方法有效，哪些无效。

While going to meetings I had already been studying why some papers are remembered and most are not. The technical person wants to give a highly limited technical talk. Most of the time the audience wants a broad general talk and wants much more survey and background than the speaker is willing to give. As a result, many talks are ineffective. The speaker names a topic and suddenly plunges into the details he's solved. Few people in the audience may follow. You should paint a general picture to say why it's important, and then slowly give a sketch of what was done. Then a larger number of people will say, \`\`Yes, Joe has done that,'' or \`\`Mary has done that; I really see where it is; yes, Mary really gave a good talk; I understand what Mary has done.'' The tendency is to give a highly restricted, safe talk; this is usually ineffective. Furthermore, many talks are filled with far too much information. So I say this idea of selling is obvious.

在参加各种会议的过程中，我一直在研究为什么有些论文能被记住，而大多数却无人问津。技术人员往往只想做内容非常有限的技术演讲。而**大多数时候，听众想要的是内容广泛、涵盖面广的演讲，他们希望获得比演讲者愿意提供的更多的背景信息和概述**。结果，很多演讲都收效甚微。演讲者提出一个主题，然后突然开始深入讲解他已经解决的细节。听众中可能很少有人能跟上。**你应该先描绘出一个大致的图景，说明它的重要性，然后再慢慢地概述具体的工作内容**。这样，更多的人会说：“是的，乔做过这个”，或者“玛丽做过这个；我真的明白了；是的，玛丽的演讲很精彩；我理解玛丽做了什么。” 人们倾向于做内容非常有限、保守的演讲；但这通常效果不佳。此外，很多演讲都包含过多的信息。所以我认为，这种推销的思路显而易见。

Let me summarize. You've got to work on important problems. I deny that it is all luck, but I admit there is a fair element of luck. I subscribe to Pasteur's \`\`Luck favors the prepared mind.'' I favor heavily what I did. Friday afternoons for years - great thoughts only - means that I committed 10% of my time trying to understand the bigger problems in the field, i.e. what was and what was not important. I found in the early days I had believed \`this' and yet had spent all week marching in \`that' direction. It was kind of foolish. If I really believe the action is over there, why do I march in this direction? I either had to change my goal or change what I did. So I changed something I did and I marched in the direction I thought was important. It's that easy.

我来总结一下。你必须专注攻克重要的难题。我不认为成功全靠运气，但我承认运气确实占据不小的比重。我认同巴斯德的名言：“机遇偏爱有准备的头脑。”我十分推崇自己过往的行事方式。多年以来，**每个周五下午，我只专注深度思考、酝酿重要想法，这意味着我会抽出百分之十的时间，去钻研所在领域的宏观核心问题，厘清何为关键、何为次要**。早年我发现，自己明明认同某一理念，整周的行动却始终朝着完全相反的方向推进，这着实荒唐可笑。如果我真心认定目标在彼处，为何还要一味朝着此处前行？我要么调整目标，要么改变自身行动。最终我选择调整行事方式，朝着自己认定重要的方向稳步前行。道理其实就这么简单。

Now you might tell me you haven't got control over what you have to work on. Well, when you first begin, you may not. But once you're moderately successful, there are more people asking for results than you can deliver and you have some power of choice, but not completely. I'll tell you a story about that, and it bears on the subject of educating your boss. I had a boss named Schelkunoff; he was, and still is, a very good friend of mine. Some military person came to me and demanded some answers by Friday. Well, I had already dedicated my computing resources to reducing data on the fly for a group of scientists; I was knee deep in short, small, important problems. This military person wanted me to solve his problem by the end of the day on Friday. I said, \`\`No, I'll give it to you Monday. I can work on it over the weekend. I'm not going to do it now.'' He goes down to my boss, Schelkunoff, and Schelkunoff says, \`\`You must run this for him; he's got to have it by Friday.'' I tell him, \`\`Why do I?''; he says, \`\`You have to.'' I said, \`\`Fine, Sergei, but you're sitting in your office Friday afternoon catching the late bus home to watch as this fellow walks out that door.'' I gave the military person the answers late Friday afternoon. I then went to Schelkunoff's office and sat down; as the man goes out I say, \`\`You see Schelkunoff, this fellow has nothing under his arm; but I gave him the answers.'' On Monday morning Schelkunoff called him up and said, \`\`Did you come in to work over the weekend?'' I could hear, as it were, a pause as the fellow ran through his mind of what was going to happen; but he knew he would have had to sign in, and he'd better not say he had when he hadn't, so he said he hadn't. Ever after that Schelkunoff said, \`\`You set your deadlines; you can change them.''

于是他去找了我的上司谢尔库诺夫。谢尔库诺夫对我说：“你必须帮他处理这件事，他周五一定要拿到结果。” 我反问：“我凭什么必须做？” 他只回复：“你就得做。” 我说道：“好的，谢尔盖。但周五下午你就坐在办公室里，等着赶末班公交回家，好好看着这个人走出大门就好。”周五傍晚，我才把答复交给那位军方人员。随后我去到谢尔库诺夫的办公室坐下，看着那人离开，我开口说道：“你看，谢尔库诺夫，这个人两手空空走了出去，但我确实按时给了他想要的答案。”周一早上，谢尔库诺夫拨通了对方的电话，问道：“你周末来公司加班了吗？” 我能隐约听见电话那头一阵沉默，对方快速盘算着后果。但他清楚，加班需要打卡登记，既然没来，就不能谎称加班，最后只能如实回答自己周末并未加班。从那以后，谢尔库诺夫便定下规矩：“工作截止时间由你来制定，并且你有权灵活调整。”

One lesson was sufficient to educate my boss as to why I didn't want to do big jobs that displaced exploratory research and why I was justified in not doing crash jobs which absorb all the research computing facilities. I wanted instead to use the facilities to compute a large number of small problems. Again, in the early days, I was limited in computing capacity and it was clear, in my area, that a \`\`mathematician had no use for machines.'' But I needed more machine capacity. Every time I had to tell some scientist in some other area, \`\`No I can't; I haven't the machine capacity,'' he complained. I said \`\`Go tell your Vice President that Hamming needs more computing capacity.'' After a while I could see what was happening up there at the top; many people said to my Vice President, \`\`Your man needs more computing capacity.'' I got it!

有一次教训就足以让我的老板明白，我为什么不想接手那些挤占探索性研究的大型项目，也有理由拒绝那些占用所有研究计算设备的紧急任务。相反，我希望利用这些设备来计算大量的小问题。同样，在早期，我的计算能力是有限的，而且在我所在的领域，很明显“数学家根本不需要机器”。但我需要更多的计算能力。每次我都得对其他领域的一些科学家说：“不行，我没有足够的计算设备。”他就会抱怨。我就说：“你去跟你们的副总裁说，哈明需要更多的计算能力。”久而久之，高层的态度开始发生转变，不少人都向我的副总反馈：“你手下的这位员工需要扩充算力。”最终，我成功争取到了足够的计算资源。

I also did a second thing. When I loaned what little programming power we had to help in the early days of computing, I said, \`\`We are not getting the recognition for our programmers that they deserve. When you publish a paper you will thank that programmer or you aren't getting any more help from me. That programmer is going to be thanked by name; she's worked hard.'' I waited a couple of years. I then went through a year of BSTJ articles and counted what fraction thanked some programmer. I took it into the boss and said, That's the central role computing is playing in Bell Labs; if the BSTJ is important, that's how important computing is. He had to give in. You can educate your bosses. It's a hard job. In this talk I'm only viewing from the bottom up; I'm not viewing from the top down. But I am telling you how you can get what you want in spite of top management. You have to sell your ideas there also.

我还做了第二件事。在计算机发展初期，我借出我们仅有的少量编程人力提供协助时，曾明确表态：“我们的程序员付出了诸多努力，却没能获得应有的认可。你们发表论文时，必须对负责开发的程序员致以感谢，否则我不会再提供任何协助。必须署上这位程序员的姓名以示致谢，她为此付出了大量心血。”我默默等待了数年，随后梳理了一整年的《贝尔系统技术期刊》刊载文章，统计其中致谢程序员的文章占比。我拿着这份数据找到上级，说道：“这恰恰体现了计算技术在贝尔实验室的核心地位；倘若这本期刊具备重要价值，那就足以说明计算领域同样至关重要。”最终，上级不得不做出让步。你完全可以去引导、说服你的管理者，只是这份工作并不容易。在本次分享中，我仅从基层视角出发展开阐述，而非站在高层角度看待问题。但我会告诉大家，**即便面对高层管理层，你依然有办法争取自己想要的资源与权益。在职场中，你同样需要主动推广、说服他人认可你的想法。**

Well I now come down to the topic, \`\`Is the effort to be a great scientist worth it?'' To answer this, you must ask people. When you get beyond their modesty, most people will say, \`\`Yes, doing really first-class work, and knowing it, is as good as wine, women and song put together,'' or if it's a woman she says, \`\`It is as good as wine, men and song put together.'' And if you look at the bosses, they tend to come back or ask for reports, trying to participate in those moments of discovery. They're always in the way. So evidently those who have done it, want to do it again. But it is a limited survey. I have never dared to go out and ask those who didn't do great work how they felt about the matter. It's a biased sample, but I still think it is worth the struggle. I think it is very definitely worth the struggle to try and do first-class work because the truth is, the value is in the struggle more than it is in the result. The struggle to make something of yourself seems to be worthwhile in itself. The success and fame are sort of dividends, in my opinion.

接下来我切入正题：“倾尽心力成为一名伟大的科学家，值得吗？”想要解答这个问题，就必须去问问世人。抛开人们的谦逊不谈，大多数人都会给出肯定的答案：“没错，做出顶尖的科研成果，并深知其价值，这份美好胜过美酒、佳人与欢歌的总和。”若是女性，则会这样表述：“这份成就的美好，堪比美酒、良人与乐曲相融。”再看那些身居高位的管理者，他们总会反复跟进工作、索要汇报，总想参与到科研探索与发现的关键时刻，却往往只会徒增阻碍。显而易见，亲身经历过这份科研求索的人，都渴望再次拥有这般体验。但我的调研范围十分有限。我从未鼓起勇气去询问那些未曾做出卓越成就的人，了解他们对此事的看法。这份调研的样本存在片面性，可我依旧坚信，这份拼搏与付出值得。我无比笃定，倾尽心血深耕、打造一流成果的奋斗之路意义非凡。究其本质，价值更多蕴藏在拼搏的过程之中，而非最终的结果。努力雕琢自我、实现自我蜕变的求索过程，本身就极具价值。在我看来，成功与名望，不过是这份奋斗附赠的额外馈赠。

I've told you how to do it. It is so easy, so why do so many people, with all their talents, fail? For example, my opinion, to this day, is that there are in the mathematics department at Bell Labs quite a few people far more able and far better endowed than I, but they didn't produce as much. Some of them did produce more than I did; Shannon produced more than I did, and some others produced a lot, but I was highly productive against a lot of other fellows who were better equipped. Why is it so? What happened to them? Why do so many of the people who have great promise, fail?

我已经告诉过你该怎么做了。这如此简单，那么为什么有那么多有才华的人会失败呢？例如，直到今天，我的观点仍然是，在贝尔实验室的数学系里，有相当多的人比我更有能力，天赋也更好，但他们的成果却没有我多。有些人确实比我做得多，香农的成果就比我多，还有其他一些人也成果颇丰，但在许多其他条件更优越的同事面前，我的产出依然很高。为什么会这样？他们出了什么问题？为什么那么多潜力巨大的人最终却失败了？

Well, one of the reasons is drive and commitment. The people who do great work with less ability but who are committed to it, get more done that those who have great skill and dabble in it, who work during the day and go home and do other things and come back and work the next day. They don't have the deep commitment that is apparently necessary for really first-class work. They turn out lots of good work, but we were talking, remember, about first-class work. There is a difference. Good people, very talented people, almost always turn out good work. We're talking about the outstanding work, the type of work that gets the Nobel Prize and gets recognition.

嗯，其中一个原因是干劲与投入。那些天赋不算出众但始终全心投入的人，比起那些身怀绝技却浅尝辄止的人，能完成更多工作——后者白天上班，回家就做别的事，第二天再回来继续工作。他们缺乏做出真正一流成果所必需的深度投入。他们能产出大量优质作品，但要知道，我们谈论的是一流的工作。这两者是有区别的。优秀的人才、极具天赋的人，几乎总是能产出优秀的工作。我们谈论的是卓越的工作，那种能斩获诺贝尔奖、获得广泛认可的作品。

The second thing is, I think, the problem of personality defects. Now I'll cite a fellow whom I met out in Irvine. He had been the head of a computing center and he was temporarily on assignment as a special assistant to the president of the university. It was obvious he had a job with a great future. He took me into his office one time and showed me his method of getting letters done and how he took care of his correspondence. He pointed out how inefficient the secretary was. He kept all his letters stacked around there; he knew where everything was. And he would, on his word processor, get the letter out. He was bragging how marvelous it was and how he could get so much more work done without the secretary's interference. Well, behind his back, I talked to the secretary. The secretary said, \`\`Of course I can't help him; I don't get his mail. He won't give me the stuff to log in; I don't know where he puts it on the floor. Of course I can't help him.'' So I went to him and said, \`\`Look, if you adopt the present method and do what you can do single-handedly, you can go just that far and no farther than you can do single-handedly. If you will learn to work with the system, you can go as far as the system will support you.'' And, he never went any further. He had his personality defect of wanting total control and was not willing to recognize that you need the support of the system.

第二点，我认为，是性格缺陷的问题。现在我要举一个我在尔湾遇到的人的例子。他曾是计算中心的负责人，当时被临时派去担任大学校长的特别助理。很明显，他的这份工作前途光明。有一次，他带我进了他的办公室，向我展示了他处理信件的方法，以及他如何处理各类通信事务。他还指出了秘书有多低效。他把所有信件都堆在周围，却清楚每样东西的位置。然后他会在文字处理软件上把信件打出来。他还得意地说这方法有多棒，没有秘书的干扰，他能完成更多的工作。 然而，我在他背后和秘书聊了聊。秘书说：“我当然帮不了他，我收不到他的邮件。他不肯把资料给我录入，我也不知道他把东西扔在地上的哪个位置。我当然帮不了他。” 于是我去找他，说道：“听着，**如果你采用现在的方法，单靠自己去做所有事，你的成就也就仅限于你个人能力所及的范围。如果你学会借助体系运作，你就能达到体系所能支撑的高度**。” 但他始终没有更进一步。他存在想要完全掌控一切的人格缺陷，也不愿承认自己需要体系的支持。

You find this happening again and again; good scientists will fight the system rather than learn to work with the system and take advantage of all the system has to offer. It has a lot, if you learn how to use it. It takes patience, but you can learn how to use the system pretty well, and you can learn how to get around it. After all, if you want a decision \`No', you just go to your boss and get a \`No' easy. If you want to do something, don't ask, do it. Present him with an accomplished fact. Don't give him a chance to tell you \`No'. But if you want a \`No', it's easy to get a \`No'.

你会发现这类情况屡见不鲜：优秀的科研工作者往往选择与现有体制对抗，而非**学着顺应体制、充分利用体制所能提供的一切资源**。只要你掌握方法，体制能给予的助力其实十分可观。这需要足够的耐心，但你完全可以熟练驾驭规则，也能学会灵活规避条条框框的束缚。说到底，若是一心只想得到否定的答复，直接去找上级，就能轻易被拒绝。**倘若你真心想做成某件事，就不要事先请示，直接付诸行动，把既成事实摆在上级面前，不给对方否决你的机会**。而如果本身就想要被拒绝，那实在是轻而易举。

Another personality defect is ego assertion and I'll speak in this case of my own experience. I came from Los Alamos and in the early days I was using a machine in New York at 590 Madison Avenue where we merely rented time. I was still dressing in western clothes, big slash pockets, a bolo and all those things. I vaguely noticed that I was not getting as good service as other people. So I set out to measure. You came in and you waited for your turn; I felt I was not getting a fair deal. I said to myself, \`\`Why? No Vice President at IBM said, \`Give Hamming a bad time'. It is the secretaries at the bottom who are doing this. When a slot appears, they'll rush to find someone to slip in, but they go out and find somebody else. Now, why? I haven't mistreated them.'' Answer, I wasn't dressing the way they felt somebody in that situation should. It came down to just that - I wasn't dressing properly. I had to make the decision - was I going to assert my ego and dress the way I wanted to and have it steadily drain my effort from my professional life, or was I going to appear to conform better? I decided I would make an effort to appear to conform properly. The moment I did, I got much better service. And now, as an old colorful character, I get better service than other people.

另一种性格缺陷是自我执念过强，我将结合自身经历来谈谈这一点。我曾就职于洛斯阿拉莫斯实验室，早年期间，我在纽约麦迪逊大道590号使用一台设备，我们只是按时长租用设备使用权。彼时我依旧常穿西部风格服饰，带有大斜插口袋、佩戴波洛领带，一身随性装扮。我隐约察觉到，自己得到的服务待遇远不如其他人。于是我开始留意观察：所有人进店后都需要排队等候，但我总觉得自己受到了不公平对待。我暗自思索：“原因何在？IBM没有任何一位副总裁特意下令刁难汉明。做出区别对待的，只是底层的秘书们。每当有空缺时段出现，她们会优先安排其他人使用，唯独刻意避开我。可我从未苛待过她们，这究竟是为什么？”答案其实很简单：我的穿着打扮，不符合她们对该场合职场人士的固有认知。归根结底，问题就出在着装不合时宜。我必须做出抉择：是坚持自我、随心穿搭，任由这种偏见持续消耗我的工作精力；还是选择顺应环境、贴合职场着装规范？最终我决定主动调整着装，融入当下的环境。而从我做出改变的那一刻起，我所获得的服务待遇立刻好了很多。如今，我已是一位风格鲜明、阅历丰富的长者，反而能收获比旁人更优厚的对待。

You should dress according to the expectations of the audience spoken to. If I am going to give an address at the MIT computer center, I dress with a bolo and an old corduroy jacket or something else. I know enough not to let my clothes, my appearance, my manners get in the way of what I care about. An enormous number of scientists feel they must assert their ego and do their thing their way. They have got to be able to do this, that, or the other thing, and they pay a steady price.

你应当根据沟通对象的预期来搭配着装。如果我要在麻省理工学院计算机中心发表演讲，我会佩戴波洛领带，搭配一件老旧的灯芯绒夹克或是其他类似服饰。我十分清楚，不能让衣着打扮、外在形象与言行举止，阻碍自己追求在意的事物。许许多多的科研工作者都认为，必须彰显自我个性，凡事坚持按自己的方式行事。他们执着于特立独行、随心所欲，而这也让他们为此付出了长久且持续的代价。

John Tukey almost always dressed very casually. He would go into an important office and it would take a long time before the other fellow realized that this is a first-class man and he had better listen. For a long time John has had to overcome this kind of hostility. It's wasted effort! I didn't say you should conform; I said \`\`The appearance of conforming gets you a long way.'' If you chose to assert your ego in any number of ways, \`\`I am going to do it my way,'' you pay a small steady price throughout the whole of your professional career. And this, over a whole lifetime, adds up to an enormous amount of needless trouble.

约翰·图基平日里穿着向来十分随意。他走进一间重要的办公场所后，对方往往要过很久才能意识到，眼前这人学识卓越、能力顶尖，理应认真倾听其话语。长久以来，约翰不得不一直克服这类因外表偏见产生的抵触与轻视。这般付出完全是徒劳之举！我并非主张人要一味迎合世俗、随波逐流，而是想说：看似合群、合乎场合的外在表现，能让人在处事中事半功倍、一路顺遂。倘若你执意以种种方式彰显自我、我行我素，秉持“凡事都要按自己的方式来”的行事准则，那么在整个职业生涯中，你都要为此持续付出细碎且长久的代价。日积月累下来，这一生便会凭空多出无数不必要的麻烦与阻碍。

By taking the trouble to tell jokes to the secretaries and being a little friendly, I got superb secretarial help. For instance, one time for some idiot reason all the reproducing services at Murray Hill were tied up. Don't ask me how, but they were. I wanted something done. My secretary called up somebody at Holmdel, hopped the company car, made the hour-long trip down and got it reproduced, and then came back. It was a payoff for the times I had made an effort to cheer her up, tell her jokes and be friendly; it was that little extra work that later paid off for me. By realizing you have to use the system and studying how to get the system to do your work, you learn how to adapt the system to your desires. Or you can fight it steadily, as a small undeclared war, for the whole of your life.

我特意费心给秘书们讲笑话，对他们多了几分友善，结果得到了她们超棒的协助。比如有一次，不知怎么回事，穆雷山校区所有的复印服务都被占满了。别问我具体原因，反正就是这样。我有件事需要处理。我的秘书给霍姆德尔校区的人打了电话，然后坐上公司的车，花了一个小时一路赶过去，把要复印的东西复印好，又赶了回来。这算是对我那些努力的回报——平时我总逗她开心、给她讲笑话、对她友善，而这点额外的付出，后来也为我带来了回报。只要懂得要利用这套系统，并且研究如何让系统为你工作，你就能学会让系统顺应自己的需求。否则，你也可以终其一生，持续和这套系统打一场不宣而战的小型战争。

And I think John Tukey paid a terrible price needlessly. He was a genius anyhow, but I think it would have been far better, and far simpler, had he been willing to conform a little bit instead of ego asserting. He is going to dress the way he wants all of the time. It applies not only to dress but to a thousand other things; people will continue to fight the system. Not that you shouldn't occasionally!

我认为约翰·图基无谓地付出了惨痛代价。他本就是一位天才，但在我看来，倘若他愿意稍微顺应规则、而非一味彰显自我，一切本会好得多，也简单得多。他向来随心所欲、我行我素地穿衣打扮。这种处事方式不仅体现在衣着上，还渗透在方方面面；人们总会执意与体制对抗。当然，偶尔反抗体制也并无不可。

When they moved the library from the middle of Murray Hill to the far end, a friend of mine put in a request for a bicycle. Well, the organization was not dumb. They waited awhile and sent back a map of the grounds saying, \`\`Will you please indicate on this map what paths you are going to take so we can get an insurance policy covering you.'' A few more weeks went by. They then asked, \`\`Where are you going to store the bicycle and how will it be locked so we can do so and so.'' He finally realized that of course he was going to be red-taped to death so he gave in. He rose to be the President of Bell Laboratories.

当初图书馆从默里山中心区域搬迁至远端后，我的一位朋友申请领用一辆自行车。要知道，这家机构一点也不糊涂。他们拖延了一段时间，随后寄回一张园区地图，并附言：“请你在这张地图上标注出日常骑行路线，以便我们为你办理专属保险。”又过了几周，对方再度发问：“你打算将自行车存放于何处？如何上锁保管？我们需要核实这些信息才能走后续流程。”他最终意识到，自己只会被繁琐的官僚手续层层刁难、耗尽精力，于是只好放弃申请。而这位朋友后来一路晋升，出任了贝尔实验室的总裁。

Barney Oliver was a good man. He wrote a letter one time to the IEEE. At that time the official shelf space at Bell Labs was so much and the height of the IEEE Proceedings at that time was larger; and since you couldn't change the size of the official shelf space he wrote this letter to the IEEE Publication person saying, \`\`Since so many IEEE members were at Bell Labs and since the official space was so high the journal size should be changed.'' He sent it for his boss's signature. Back came a carbon with his signature, but he still doesn't know whether the original was sent or not. I am not saying you shouldn't make gestures of reform. I am saying that my study of able people is that they don't get themselves _committed_ to that kind of warfare. They play it a little bit and drop it and get on with their work.

巴尼·奥利弗是个正直善良的人。他曾给IEEE写过一封信。彼时，贝尔实验室的标准书架层高固定，而《IEEE汇刊》的开本高度偏高；由于标准书架尺寸无法改动，他便致信该协会的出版负责人，信中写道：“鉴于贝尔实验室有大量协会会员，且现有标准书架层高有限，理应调整期刊的开本尺寸。”他将信件上交，等待上司签字批复。最后拿回了一份带有上司签名的副本，但他至今都不清楚，信件原件究竟有没有被寄出。我并非反对人们做出改革的姿态。但根据我对优秀能干之人的观察，他们不会让自己深陷这类无意义的内耗与纷争之中。他们只会浅尝辄止，随即抽身抽身，专心投入自身的本职工作。

Many a second-rate fellow gets caught up in some little twitting of the system, and carries it through to warfare. He expends his energy in a foolish project. Now you are going to tell me that somebody has to change the system. I agree; somebody's has to. Which do you want to be? The person who changes the system or the person who does first-class science? Which person is it that you want to be? Be clear, when you fight the system and struggle with it, what you are doing, how far to go out of amusement, and how much to waste your effort fighting the system. My advice is to let somebody else do it and you get on with becoming a first-class scientist. Very few of you have the ability to both reform the system _and_ become a first-class scientist.

许多平庸之辈总会纠结于对体制的些许不满与嘲讽，继而将这种抵触演变为无休止的对抗。他们把精力耗费在毫无意义的无谓抗争上。或许你会反驳我，总需要有人去变革现有体制。对此我深表认同，变革之事的确需要有人去做。但你要想清楚，你想成为哪一类人？**是耗费心力改造体制的人，还是深耕科研、成就顶尖学术造诣的科学家？你真正想要成为的，究竟是哪一种人**？当你选择与现有体制抗衡、陷入内耗博弈时，务必认清自身行为的本质，分清适度吐槽消遣和全力对抗的边界，衡量好究竟要耗费多少精力去纠结体制问题。我的建议是，把体制变革的事交由他人去做，专注自身发展，努力成长为一名顶尖科学家。绝大多数人，并不具备同时兼顾体制改革与顶尖科研事业的双重能力。

On the other hand, we can't always give in. There are times when a certain amount of rebellion is sensible. I have observed almost all scientists enjoy a certain amount of twitting the system for the sheer love of it. What it comes down to basically is that you cannot be original in one area without having originality in others. Originality is being different. You can't be an original scientist without having some other original characteristics. But many a scientist has let his quirks in other places make him pay a far higher price than is necessary for the ego satisfaction he or she gets. I'm not against all ego assertion; I'm against some.

但另一方面，我们也不能一味妥协。有些时候，适度的反抗是合乎情理的。我发现，几乎所有科学家都乐于单纯出于本心去调侃、质疑现有体制。归根结底，一个人倘若只在单一领域拥有独创思维，便算不上真正具备创新能力。创新，本质就是与众不同。一名极具独创性的科学家，必然会在其他方面拥有独特特质。然而，不少科学家因自身别处的特立独行与古怪行事，为了换取一时的自我满足，付出了远超必要的沉重代价。我并非反对所有的自我主张与个性彰显，只是不认同部分毫无意义的自我标榜。

Another fault is anger. Often a scientist becomes angry, and this is no way to handle things. Amusement, yes, anger, no. Anger is misdirected. You should follow and cooperate rather than struggle against the system all the time.

另一个缺点是愤怒。科学家常常会发怒，而这绝不是处理问题的方式。可以保持幽默，但绝不能愤怒。愤怒是用错了地方。你应该顺应并配合系统，而不是一直与系统对抗。

Another thing you should look for is the positive side of things instead of the negative. I have already given you several examples, and there are many, many more; how, given the situation, by changing the way I looked at it, I converted what was apparently a defect to an asset. I'll give you another example. I am an egotistical person; there is no doubt about it. I knew that most people who took a sabbatical to write a book, didn't finish it on time. So before I left, I told all my friends that when I come back, that book was going to be done! Yes, I would have it done - I'd have been ashamed to come back without it! I used my ego to make myself behave the way I wanted to. I bragged about something so I'd have to perform. I found out many times, like a cornered rat in a real trap, I was surprisingly capable. I have found that it paid to say, \`\`Oh yes, I'll get the answer for you Tuesday,'' not having any idea how to do it. By Sunday night I was really hard thinking on how I was going to deliver by Tuesday. I often put my pride on the line and sometimes I failed, but as I said, like a cornered rat I'm surprised how often I did a good job. I think you need to learn to use yourself. I think you need to know how to convert a situation from one view to another which would increase the chance of success.

你还需要学会多看事物积极的一面，而非纠结于消极之处。我已经给你举过不少例子，类似的例子还有数不胜数：身处某种境遇时，我通过转变看待问题的视角，将原本看似的短板转化成了自身的优势。我再举一个例子。我本身是个自负的人，这一点毋庸置疑。我了解到，大多数休假潜心写书的人，最终都无法按时完稿。于是在休假离开前，我向所有朋友宣称，等我归来之时，这本书必定顺利完成。没错，我一定要写完这本书，若是空手而归，我会倍感羞愧。我利用自身的自负心理，倒逼自己按照理想的方式行事。我刻意夸下海口，迫使自己必须兑现承诺。无数次经历让我发现，**人就像被逼入绝境、困在陷阱中的老鼠一般，往往能爆发出超乎想象的能力**。我还发现，即便完全没有头绪，随口答应别人“没问题，周二我就给你答复”，往往会收获不错的结果。到了周日晚上，我便会绞尽脑汁，想方设法在周二如期交出成果。我常常将自己的自尊心置于风口浪尖，偶尔也会遭遇失败。但正如我所说，如同绝境求生一般，我屡屡出色地完成了挑战，连自己都感到意外。我认为，人要学会合理利用自身的特质，学会切换看待处境的角度，以此提升获得成功的概率。

Now self-delusion in humans is very, very common. There are enumerable ways of you changing a thing and kidding yourself and making it look some other way. When you ask, \`\`Why didn't you do such and such,'' the person has a thousand alibis. If you look at the history of science, usually these days there are 10 people right there ready, and we pay off for the person who is there first. The other nine fellows say, \`\`Well, I had the idea but I didn't do it and so on and so on.'' There are so many alibis. Why weren't you first? Why didn't you do it right? Don't try an alibi. Don't try and kid yourself. You can tell other people all the alibis you want. I don't mind. But to yourself try to be honest.

自我欺骗在人类身上极为普遍。人们总能想出无数种方式，曲解事实、自我蒙蔽，刻意扭曲事物的本来样貌。当你质问他人：“你当初为何没有完成这件事、做好那件事？”对方总能找出千百种借口。纵观科学发展的历程，如今往往会有十人同时酝酿同一个研究方向，最终只有率先取得成果的人能够收获荣誉与回报。其余九人便会辩解：“我早就想到了这个思路，只是迟迟没有付诸行动”，诸如此类的说辞层出不穷。世人总有无数借口为自己开脱。为何你没能抢占先机？为何你没能把事情做好？**不要再寻找借口，不要再自我欺骗**。你可以向旁人编织各种理由，我对此毫无所谓。但面对自己，请务必保持坦诚。

If you really want to be a first-class scientist you need to know yourself, your weaknesses, your strengths, and your bad faults, like my egotism. How can you convert a fault to an asset? How can you convert a situation where you haven't got enough manpower to move into a direction when that's exactly what you need to do? I say again that I have seen, as I studied the history, the successful scientist changed the viewpoint and what was a defect became an asset.

如果你真心想成为一流的科学家，就**必须认清自我，了解自身的短板、优势与缺点**，就像我自身的自负一样。你要如何将缺点转化为优势？当亟需朝着某个方向推进工作，却面临人手不足的困境时，又该如何破局？我再次强调，在研究历史的过程中我发现，那些取得成功的科学家，都会转变看待问题的视角，从而让自身的短板化作独特的优势。

In summary, I claim that some of the reasons why so many people who have greatness within their grasp don't succeed are: they don't work on important problems, they don't become emotionally involved, they don't try and change what is difficult to some other situation which is easily done but is still important, and they keep giving themselves alibis why they don't. They keep saying that it is a matter of luck. I've told you how easy it is; furthermore I've told you how to reform. Therefore, go forth and become great scientists!

总而言之，我认为许多本可成就非凡的人最终未能取得成功，原因主要有以下几点：他们**不致力于重要问题，不全身心投入，不愿将棘手的难题转化为易于着手却依旧具备价值的研究方向，还总是为自己的不作为寻找借口**。他们一味将成败归咎于运气。我早已告诉过大家，通往卓越的道路并不艰难，也讲解过自我革新的方法。因此，勇敢前行，去成为优秀的科学家吧！

(End of the formal part of the talk.)
（演讲的正式部分结束。）

讨论 - 问答**

A. G. Chynoweth: Well that was 50 minutes of concentrated wisdom and observations accumulated over a fantastic career; I lost track of all the observations that were striking home. Some of them are very very timely. One was the plea for more computer capacity; I was hearing nothing but that this morning from several people, over and over again. So that was right on the mark today even though here we are 20 - 30 years after when you were making similar remarks, Dick. I can think of all sorts of lessons that all of us can draw from your talk. And for one, as I walk around the halls in the future I hope I won't see as many closed doors in Bellcore. That was one observation I thought was very intriguing.
A. G. Chynoweth：这真是 50 分钟的集中智慧与观察积累，是你辉煌职业生涯的精华；我几乎没注意到所有那些令人深思的观察。其中一些非常及时。一个是呼吁更多计算机能力；今天早上我听到的就是这些，反复听。所以今天你说得非常对，尽管我们比你说类似话的时候晚了 20-30 年，迪克。我能想到所有我们能从你的谈话中吸取的教训。首先，未来当我走遍贝尔 core 的大楼时，我希望我不会看到太多紧闭的门。这是我认为非常引人入胜的一个观察。

非常非常感谢你，迪克；那真是美好的回忆。现在我将开放提问环节。我相信有很多人想就迪克提到的某些观点进行讨论。

_Hamming:_ First let me respond to Alan Chynoweth about computing. I had computing in research and for 10 years I kept telling my management, \`\`Get that !&@#% machine out of research. We are being forced to run problems all the time. We can't do research because were too busy operating and running the computing machines.'' Finally the message got through. They were going to move computing out of research to someplace else. I was persona non grata to say the least and I was surprised that people didn't kick my shins because everybody was having their toy taken away from them. I went in to Ed David's office and said, \`\`Look Ed, you've got to give your researchers a machine. If you give them a great big machine, we'll be back in the same trouble we were before, so busy keeping it going we can't think. Give them the smallest machine you can because they are very able people. They will learn how to do things on a small machine instead of mass computing.'' As far as I'm concerned, that's how UNIX arose. We gave them a moderately small machine and they decided to make it do great things. They had to come up with a system to do it on. It is called UNIX!
汉明：首先，我来回应艾伦·奇诺韦思关于计算业务的问题。当时研究部门配备了计算设备，整整十年间，我不断向管理层反映：“把那台破机器搬出研究部门！我们被迫日复一日处理各类运算任务，终日忙于操作和维护计算设备，根本没有精力开展研究工作。”最终，我的诉求被采纳。公司计划将计算业务从研究部门剥离，迁移至其他场地。毫不夸张地说，我因此成了众人排斥的对象，甚至诧异没人当面刁难我，毕竟所有人都相当于被夺走了心爱的工具。我走进埃德·戴维的办公室，对他说：“埃德，你必须给研究人员配备一台设备。如果给他们一台大型高性能机器，我们会重蹈覆辙，所有人都会疲于维持设备运转，无暇深度思考。尽量给他们配置精简小型的设备就好，这些研究人员能力出众，他们会学会依托小型设备完成工作，而非依赖大规模集中计算。”在我看来，这正是UNIX系统诞生的契机。我们为研究团队配备了一台体量适中的小型设备，而他们决心凭借这台设备创造非凡价值。为此，他们不得不研发一套全新的操作系统，这套系统，就是后来的UNIX！

_A. G. Chynoweth:_ I just have to pick up on that one. In our present environment, Dick, while we wrestle with some of the red tape attributed to, or required by, the regulators, there is one quote that one exasperated AVP came up with and I've used it over and over again. He growled that, \`\`UNIX was never a deliverable!''
A.G. Chynoweth：我必须就这一点提一下。迪克，在我们目前的环境下，尽管我们要应对监管机构造成或要求的一些繁文缛节，但有一位恼怒的副总裁说过一句话，我反复用过很多次。他怒气冲冲地说：“UNIX 从来就不是一个可交付的产品！”

_Question:_ What about personal stress? Does that seem to make a difference?
问题：那个人压力呢？这似乎有影响吗？

_Hamming:_ Yes, it does. If you don't get emotionally involved, it doesn't. I had incipient ulcers most of the years that I was at Bell Labs. I have since gone off to the Naval Postgraduate School and laid back somewhat, and now my health is much better. But if you want to be a great scientist you're going to have to put up with stress. You can lead a nice life; you can be a nice guy or you can be a great scientist. But nice guys end last, is what Leo Durocher said. If you want to lead a nice happy life with a lot of recreation and everything else, you'll lead a nice life.
哈明：是的，确实如此。如果你不涉及感情，那就没事。我在贝尔实验室工作的那些年大部分时间都有潜在的胃溃疡。后来我去了海军研究生院，有所放松，现在健康状况好多了。但如果你想成为一位伟大的科学家，你将不得不忍受压力。你可以过上美好的生活；你可以做一个好人，或者成为一个伟大的科学家。但正如里奥·杜罗歇所说，好人最后会失败。如果你想要过上美好的幸福生活，有很多娱乐和其他一切，你会过上美好的生活。

_Question:_ The remarks about having courage, no one could argue with; but those of us who have gray hairs or who are well established don't have to worry too much. But what I sense among the young people these days is a real concern over the risk taking in a highly competitive environment. Do you have any words of wisdom on this?
问题：关于要有勇气，没有人会争论；但是那些头发花白或事业有成的人不必过于担心。但在我看来，现在的年轻人对高度竞争环境中的冒险行为确实感到担忧。对此，您有什么金玉良言吗？

_Hamming:_ I'll quote Ed David more. Ed David was concerned about the general loss of nerve in our society. It does seem to me that we've gone through various periods. Coming out of the war, coming out of Los Alamos where we built the bomb, coming out of building the radars and so on, there came into the mathematics department, and the research area, a group of people with a lot of guts. They've just seen things done; they've just won a war which was fantastic. We had reasons for having courage and therefore we did a great deal. I can't arrange that situation to do it again. I cannot blame the present generation for not having it, but I agree with what you say; I just cannot attach blame to it. It doesn't seem to me they have the desire for greatness; they lack the courage to do it. But we had, because we were in a favorable circumstance to have it; we just came through a tremendously successful war. In the war we were looking very, very bad for a long while; it was a very desperate struggle as you well know. And our success, I think, gave us courage and self confidence; that's why you see, beginning in the late forties through the fifties, a tremendous productivity at the labs which was stimulated from the earlier times. Because many of us were earlier forced to learn other things - we were forced to learn the things we didn't want to learn, we were forced to have an open door - and then we could exploit those things we learned. It is true, and I can't do anything about it; I cannot blame the present generation either. It's just a fact.
汉明：我再多引用一下埃德·戴维的话。埃德·戴维曾担忧我们社会中普遍的勇气丧失。在我看来，我们确实经历过不同时期。从战争中走出，从洛斯阿拉莫斯（我们制造原子弹的地方）走出，从建造雷达等等中走出，数学系和研究领域都涌入了一批充满胆识的人。他们亲眼见证了伟业；他们刚刚赢得了一场伟大的战争。我们拥有勇气的理由，因此我们做了很多。我无法重现那种情况。我无法责怪当代人缺乏这种品质，但我同意你的观点；我只是无法责备他们。在我看来，他们缺乏追求卓越的愿望；他们缺乏行动的勇气。但我们曾经拥有，因为我们处于能够拥有它的有利环境；我们刚刚经历了一场极其成功的战争。在战争中，我们很长一段时间都处境非常糟糕；正如你很清楚的那样，那是一场极其绝望的斗争。 我们的成功，我认为，给了我们勇气和自信心；这就是为什么从四十年代末到五十年代，你看到实验室里出现了巨大的生产力，这是从早期就开始受到刺激的。因为我们中的许多人早期被迫学习其他东西——我们被迫学习我们不想学的东西，我们被迫保持门户开放——然后我们才能利用我们学到的东西。这是事实，我也无能为力；我也不能责怪现在这一代。这只是个事实。

_Question:_ Is there something management could or should do?
问题：管理层可以做或应该做些什么？

_Hamming:_ Management can do very little. If you want to talk about managing research, that's a totally different talk. I'd take another hour doing that. This talk is about how the individual gets very successful research done in spite of anything the management does or in spite of any other opposition. And how do you do it? Just as I observe people doing it. It's just that simple and that hard!
汉明：管理层能做的事情非常有限。如果你想谈论管理研究，那完全是另一回事。我需要再花一个小时来讲那个。这次谈话是关于个人如何在管理层做任何事情或任何其他反对的情况下取得非常成功的科研。那么，你是如何做到的？就像我观察人们那样做。它就是这么简单，又这么困难！

_Question:_ Is brainstorming a daily process?
问题：头脑风暴是一个日常过程吗？

_Hamming:_ Once that was a very popular thing, but it seems not to have paid off. For myself I find it desirable to talk to other people; but a session of brainstorming is seldom worthwhile. I do go in to strictly talk to somebody and say, \`\`Look, I think there has to be something here. Here's what I think I see ...'' and then begin talking back and forth. But you want to pick capable people. To use another analogy, you know the idea called the \`critical mass.' If you have enough stuff you have critical mass. There is also the idea I used to call \`sound absorbers'. When you get too many sound absorbers, you give out an idea and they merely say, \`\`Yes, yes, yes.'' What you want to do is get that critical mass in action; \`\`Yes, that reminds me of so and so,'' or, \`\`Have you thought about that or this?'' When you talk to other people, you want to get rid of those sound absorbers who are nice people but merely say, \`\`Oh yes,'' and to find those who will stimulate you right back.
汉明：这曾经是非常流行的做法，但似乎并没有取得成效。就我个人而言，我觉得和其他人交流是很有必要的；但一场头脑风暴会议往往没什么价值。我确实会专门去找某个人谈，说：“你看，我觉得这里肯定有什么门道。这是我认为我看到的情况……”然后就开始来回交流。但你需要选择有能力的人。换个比喻来说，你知道那个叫“临界质量”的概念。如果有足够的资源，就能形成临界质量。我以前还用过另一个概念叫“吸音板”。当你身边有太多吸音板时，你提出一个想法，他们只会说“是是是”。你真正想做的是让临界质量发挥作用；听到的回应应该是“对，这让我想到了某某事”，或者“你有没有考虑过那个或这个？”**当你和别人交流时，你要摆脱那些人很好但只会说“哦，是啊”的吸音板，去找到那些能立刻激发你灵感的人**。

For example, you couldn't talk to John Pierce without being stimulated very quickly. There were a group of other people I used to talk with. For example there was Ed Gilbert; I used to go down to his office regularly and ask him questions and listen and come back stimulated. I picked my people carefully with whom I did or whom I didn't brainstorm because the sound absorbers are a curse. They are just nice guys; they fill the whole space and they contribute nothing except they absorb ideas and the new ideas just die away instead of echoing on. Yes, I find it necessary to talk to people. I think people with closed doors fail to do this so they fail to get their ideas sharpened, such as \`\`Did you ever notice something over here?'' I never knew anything about it - I can go over and look. Somebody points the way. On my visit here, I have already found several books that I must read when I get home. I talk to people and ask questions when I think they can answer me and give me clues that I do not know about. I go out and look!
比如，你只要和约翰·皮尔斯交谈，很快就会受到启发。我过去还和一群人交流过。比如埃德·吉尔伯特，我以前经常去他的办公室，向他请教问题、倾听他的见解，回来后也备受启发。我会谨慎选择和谁一起头脑风暴、不和谁头脑风暴，因为那些只会吸收想法的人简直是祸害。他们都是好人，却占满了交流的空间，毫无贡献，只会吸收想法，新的想法刚冒出来就没了，没法继续延展。没错，我觉得和人交流是必要的。我认为那些闭门造车的人做不到这一点，所以他们的想法得不到打磨。比如“你有没有注意到这边的某个情况？”我对此一无所知——我就可以过去看看。有人会为我指明方向。这次来访，我已经发现了好几本回家后必须读的书。我会主动和人交流，在觉得他们能解答我的疑问、给我提供未知线索时提出问题。我还会走出去，亲自去观察！

_Question:_ What kind of tradeoffs did you make in allocating your time for reading and writing and actually doing research?
问题：你在分配阅读、写作和实际做研究的时间上做出了什么样的权衡？

_Hamming:_ I believed, in my early days, that you should spend at least as much time in the polish and presentation as you did in the original research. Now at least 50% of the time must go for the presentation. It's a big, big number.
哈明：在我早年，我就认为你花在打磨和展示研究成果上的时间，至少应该和做原始研究的时间一样多。如今，至少要有50%的时间用于成果展示，这是一个相当可观的比例。

_Question:_ How much effort should go into library work?
问题：应该投入多少精力在图书馆工作上？

_Hamming:_ It depends upon the field. I will say this about it. There was a fellow at Bell Labs, a very, very, smart guy. He was always in the library; he read everything. If you wanted references, you went to him and he gave you all kinds of references. But in the middle of forming these theories, I formed a proposition: there would be no effect named after him in the long run. He is now retired from Bell Labs and is an Adjunct Professor. He was very valuable; I'm not questioning that. He wrote some very good Physical Review articles; but there's no effect named after him because he read too much. If you read all the time what other people have done you will think the way they thought. If you want to think new thoughts that are different, then do what a lot of creative people do - get the problem reasonably clear and then refuse to look at any answers until you've thought the problem through carefully how you would do it, how you could slightly change the problem to be the correct one. So yes, you need to keep up. You need to keep up more to find out what the problems are than to read to find the solutions. The reading is necessary to know what is going on and what is possible. But reading to get the solutions does not seem to be the way to do great research. So I'll give you two answers. You read; but it is not the amount, it is the way you read that counts.
汉明：这取决于领域。关于这一点，我可以这么说。贝尔实验室有一位同事，非常非常聪明。他总是待在图书馆里，读遍了所有东西。如果你需要参考文献，就去找他，他会给你各种参考文献。但在形成这些理论的过程中，我提出了一个命题：从长远来看，不会有以他的名字命名的效应。他现在已经从贝尔实验室退休，成为了一名兼职教授。他确实非常有价值，我对此毫不怀疑。他还在《物理评论》上发表过几篇非常出色的文章，但却没有以他名字命名的成果，原因就是他读得太多了。如果你总是去读别人做过的研究，你的思维就会和他们如出一辙。如果你想产生全新的、与众不同的思考，那就学学很多有创造力的人——**把问题弄个大致清楚，然后在你认真思考过该如何解决问题、如何稍作调整让问题变得更合适之前，拒绝去看任何现成的答案**。所以没错，你需要与时俱进。**你更需要去了解问题是什么，而不是通过阅读去找解决方案**。阅读是必要的，它能让你知晓当下的进展和可行的方向。但为了找答案而阅读，似乎并非做出卓越研究的途径。所以我给你两个答案。你确实需要阅读，但关键不在于读的数量，而在于你阅读的方式。

_Question:_ How do you get your name attached to things?
问题：你如何让你的名字与某些事物关联起来？

_Hamming:_ By doing great work. I'll tell you the hamming window one. I had given Tukey a hard time, quite a few times, and I got a phone call from him from Princeton to me at Murray Hill. I knew that he was writing up power spectra and he asked me if I would mind if he called a certain window a \`\`Hamming window.'' And I said to him, \`\`Come on, John; you know perfectly well I did only a small part of the work but you also did a lot.'' He said, \`\`Yes, Hamming, but you contributed a lot of small things; you're entitled to some credit.'' So he called it the hamming window. Now, let me go on. I had twitted John frequently about true greatness. I said true greatness is when your name is like ampere, watt, and fourier - when it's spelled with a lower case letter. That's how the hamming window came about.
汉明：通过做出出色的工作。我来跟你讲讲汉明窗的由来。我曾多次让图基很为难，有一次他从普林斯顿给我在默里山的办公室打了电话。我知道他在撰写功率谱相关的内容，他问我是否介意将某一种窗函数命名为“汉明窗”。我对他说：“别这样，约翰。你很清楚我只做了一小部分工作，而你也付出了很多。”他说：“没错，汉明，但你贡献了许多小细节；你理应获得这份荣誉。”于是他就将其命名为汉明窗。接下来我继续说。我还经常调侃约翰关于“真正的伟大”的看法。我说，真正的伟大是指你的名字像安培、瓦特、傅里叶那样——连拼写都是小写字母。汉明窗就是这样得名的。

_Question:_ Dick, would you care to comment on the relative effectiveness between giving talks, writing papers, and writing books?
问题：迪克，您能否评论一下在发表演讲、写论文和写书之间的相对效果？

_Hamming:_ In the short-haul, papers are very important if you want to stimulate someone tomorrow. If you want to get recognition long-haul, it seems to me writing books is more contribution because most of us need orientation. In this day of practically infinite knowledge, we need orientation to find our way. Let me tell you what infinite knowledge is. Since from the time of Newton to now, we have come close to doubling knowledge every 17 years, more or less. And we cope with that, essentially, by specialization. In the next 340 years at that rate, there will be 20 doublings, i.e. a million, and there will be a million fields of specialty for every one field now. It isn't going to happen. The present growth of knowledge will choke itself off until we get different tools. I believe that books which try to digest, coordinate, get rid of the duplication, get rid of the less fruitful methods and present the underlying ideas clearly of what we know now, will be the things the future generations will value. Public talks are necessary; private talks are necessary; written papers are necessary. But I am inclined to believe that, in the long-haul, books which leave out what's not essential are more important than books which tell you everything because you don't want to know everything. I don't want to know that much about penguins is the usual reply. You just want to know the essence.
汉明：从短期来看，如果你希望在短期内启发他人，论文至关重要。但若是想获得长久的认可，在我看来，著书立说的价值与贡献更大，因为我们大多数人都需要方向指引。如今知识近乎无限爆炸，我们急需指引来找准前行的方向。我来解释一下何为无限的知识。自牛顿时代至今，人类的知识总量大约每十七年就会翻一番。而我们应对知识爆炸的核心方式，就是学科细分与专业深耕。按照这个增速，在未来三百四十年里，知识总量会完成二十次翻倍，也就是增至百万倍，现如今的每一个学科领域，届时都会衍生出上百万个细分专业方向。但这是不可能实现的。在我们找到全新的认知工具之前，当下这种无序的知识扩张最终会陷入停滞。我认为，**那些试图梳理、整合知识，剔除重复内容和低效方法，并清晰呈现我们现有知识核心思想的书籍，会被后代所珍视**。公开演讲是必要的，私下交流是必要的，撰写论文也是必要的。但我倾向于认为，从长远来看，精简冗余、聚焦核心的书籍，远比事无巨细、全盘罗列内容的书籍更有价值，毕竟没有人需要掌握世间所有知识。就像人们常说的，没人想要了解企鹅的所有细节，大家只需要掌握核心关键内容即可。

_Question:_ You mentioned the problem of the Nobel Prize and the subsequent notoriety of what was done to some of the careers. Isn't that kind of a much more broad problem of fame? What can one do?
问题：你提到了诺贝尔奖的问题以及随之而来的某些职业生涯所遭受的损害。这不是一种更广泛的名声问题吗？我们能做些什么？

_Hamming:_ Some things you could do are the following. Somewhere around every seven years make a significant, if not complete, shift in your field. Thus, I shifted from numerical analysis, to hardware, to software, and so on, periodically, because you tend to use up your ideas. When you go to a new field, you have to start over as a baby. You are no longer the big mukity muk and you can start back there and you can start planting those acorns which will become the giant oaks. Shannon, I believe, ruined himself. In fact when he left Bell Labs, I said, \`\`That's the end of Shannon's scientific career.'' I received a lot of flak from my friends who said that Shannon was just as smart as ever. I said, \`\`Yes, he'll be just as smart, but that's the end of his scientific career,'' and I truly believe it was.
哈明：你可以做的一些事如下。**大约每七年，就在你的领域做出一次重大（即便不是彻底的）转变**。因此，我会定期从数值分析转向硬件，再转向软件等等，**因为你的想法往往会被用尽。当你进入一个新领域时，你就得像新手一样从头开始**。你不再是那个举足轻重的人物，你可以回到起点，播下那些终将长成参天大树的橡子。我认为香农毁了自己。事实上，当他离开贝尔实验室时，我说：“那就是香农科研生涯的终结。”我的朋友们对此颇有微词，说香农依旧和以往一样聪明。我说：“没错，他会一直这么聪明，但那就是他科学生涯的终点了。”我对此深信不疑。

You have to change. You get tired after a while; you use up your originality in one field. You need to get something nearby. I'm not saying that you shift from music to theoretical physics to English literature; I mean within your field you should shift areas so that you don't go stale. You couldn't get away with forcing a change every seven years, but if you could, I would require a condition for doing research, being that you _will_ change your field of research every seven years with a reasonable definition of what it means, or at the end of 10 years, management has the right to compel you to change. I would insist on a change because I'm serious. What happens to the old fellows is that they get a technique going; they keep on using it. They were marching in that direction which was right then, but the world changes. There's the new direction; but the old fellows are still marching in their former direction.
你必须做出改变。过一阵子你就会感到疲惫；你会在某个领域耗尽自己的创造力。你需要接触一些新的东西。我不是说你要从音乐转到理论物理再转到英国文学；我的意思是，**在你自己的领域内，你应该转换研究方向，这样才不会变得停滞不前**。你不可能做到每七年就强行换一次方向，但如果可以的话，我会给研究设定一个条件：**你必须每七年更换一次研究领域，并且要对“更换”给出合理的定义**；否则十年后，管理层有权强制你更换领域。我会坚持要求这样的改变，因为我是认真的。老一辈研究者的问题在于，他们掌握了一项技术后，就一直反复使用。他们当时沿着正确的方向前行，但世界却在变化。新的方向已经出现，可老一辈研究者仍在沿着原来的方向前进。

You need to get into a new field to get new viewpoints, and _before_ you use up all the old ones. You can do something about this, but it takes effort and energy. It takes courage to say, \`\`Yes, I will give up my great reputation.'' For example, when error correcting codes were well launched, having these theories, I said, \`\`Hamming, you are going to quit reading papers in the field; you are going to ignore it completely; you are going to try and do something else other than coast on that.'' I deliberately refused to go on in that field. I wouldn't even read papers to try to force myself to have a chance to do something else. I managed myself, which is what I'm preaching in this whole talk. Knowing many of my own faults, I manage myself. I have a lot of faults, so I've got a lot of problems, i.e. a lot of possibilities of management.
你需要在耗尽所有旧观点之前，踏入一个新领域以获取新的视角。对此你可以采取行动，但这需要付出努力和精力。说出“是的，我要放弃我显赫的声望”是需要勇气的。比如，当纠错码理论刚刚成熟问世时，我就对自己说：“汉明，你要停止阅读该领域的论文，彻底置之不理，你要尝试去做些别的事情，而不是靠着这项成就混日子。”我刻意不再继续深耕那个领域。我甚至连论文都不读，以此强迫自己有机会去做其他事。我一直在自我管理，这也是我整场演讲所倡导的。我清楚自己的诸多缺点，所以我会进行自我管理。我有很多缺点，因此也面临着很多问题，也就是有很多需要自我管理的地方。

_Question:_ Would you compare research and management?
问题：你会比较研究和管理吗？

_Hamming:_ If you want to be a great researcher, you won't make it being president of the company. If you want to be president of the company, that's another thing. I'm not against being president of the company. I just don't want to be. I think Ian Ross does a good job as President of Bell Labs. I'm not against it; but you have to be clear on what you want. Furthermore, when you're young, you may have picked wanting to be a great scientist, but as you live longer, you may change your mind. For instance, I went to my boss, Bode, one day and said, \`\`Why did you ever become department head? Why didn't you just be a good scientist?'' He said, \`\`Hamming, I had a vision of what mathematics should be in Bell Laboratories. And I saw if that vision was going to be realized, _I_ had to make it happen; _I_ had to be department head.'' When your vision of what you want to do is what you can do single-handedly, then you should pursue it. The day your vision, what you think needs to be done, is bigger than what you can do single-handedly, then you have to move toward management. And the bigger the vision is, the farther in management you have to go. If you have a vision of what the whole laboratory should be, or the whole Bell System, you have to get there to make it happen. You can't make it happen from the bottom very easily. It depends upon what goals and what desires you have. And as they change in life, you have to be prepared to change. I chose to avoid management because I preferred to do what I could do single-handedly. But that's the choice that I made, and it is biased. Each person is entitled to their choice. Keep an open mind. But when you do choose a path, for heaven's sake be aware of what you have done and the choice you have made. Don't try to do both sides.
汉明：如果你想成为一名伟大的研究员，那你当公司总裁是成不了事的。如果你想当公司总裁，那就是另一回事了。我不是反对当公司总裁，我只是不想当。我觉得伊恩·罗斯担任贝尔实验室总裁做得很好。我并不反对这个职位，但你必须清楚自己想要什么。此外，年轻时你可能立志要成为一名伟大的科学家，但随着年岁增长，你的想法或许会改变。比如，有一天我去找我的老板博德，问他：“你当初为什么要当部门主管？为什么不做一名优秀的科学家呢？”他说：“汉明，我曾构想过数学在贝尔实验室里应该是什么样子。我明白，要让这个构想成为现实，我就得亲自去实现它；所以我必须成为部门主管。”当你对自己想做的事的愿景，仅凭一己之力就能完成时，你就应该去追求它。当你的愿景——你认为需要去做的事——超出了你单枪匹马能完成的范围时，你就必须走向管理岗位。而且愿景越大，你在管理道路上就要走得越远。如果你对整个实验室，乃至整个贝尔系统应该是什么样有愿景，那你就得走到相应的位置去实现它。从底层去实现它绝非易事。这取决于你有什么样的目标和愿望。而当生活中的这些目标和愿望发生变化时，你就得做好改变的准备。我选择避开管理岗位，因为我更愿意做自己单枪匹马就能完成的事。但这只是我的选择，难免带有个人倾向。每个人都有权做出自己的选择。保持开放的心态，但一旦选定了道路，拜托务必清楚自己做了什么、选择了什么。不要试图两边都占。

_Question:_ How important is one's own expectation or how important is it to be in a group or surrounded by people who expect great work from you?
问题：个人的期望有多重要，或者身处期望你做出杰出成就的群体或被周围的人期望有多重要？

_Hamming:_ At Bell Labs everyone expected good work from me - it was a big help. Everybody expects you to do a good job, so you do, if you've got pride. I think it's very valuable to have first-class people around. I sought out the best people. The moment that physics table lost the best people, I left. The moment I saw that the same was true of the chemistry table, I left. I tried to go with people who had great ability so I could learn from them and who would expect great results out of me. By deliberately managing myself, I think I did much better than laissez faire.
哈明：在贝尔实验室，每个人都对我寄予厚望——这对我帮助很大。大家都期待你能把工作做好，所以只要你有自尊心，就会努力做到。我认为身边有一流的人才是非常宝贵的。我一直寻找最优秀的人。当那个物理研究小组失去了最优秀的人才时，我就离开了。当我发现化学研究小组也是如此时，我也离开了。**我一直想和能力出众的人共事，这样我可以向他们学习，也能促使自己拿出出色的成果**。通过主动管理自己，我认为我的表现远比放任自流要好得多。

_Question:_ You, at the outset of your talk, minimized or played down luck; but you seemed also to gloss over the circumstances that got you to Los Alamos, that got you to Chicago, that got you to Bell Laboratories.
问题：你在演讲一开始就淡化甚至忽略了运气的作用；但你似乎也刻意不提那些让你去到洛斯阿拉莫斯、去到芝加哥、去到贝尔实验室的背景情况。

_Hamming:_ There was some luck. On the other hand I don't know the alternate branches. Until you can say that the other branches would not have been equally or more successful, I can't say. Is it luck the particular thing you do? For example, when I met Feynman at Los Alamos, I knew he was going to get a Nobel Prize. I didn't know what for. But I knew darn well he was going to do great work. No matter what directions came up in the future, this man would do great work. And sure enough, he did do great work. It isn't that you only do a little great work at this circumstance and that was luck, there are many opportunities sooner or later. There are a whole pail full of opportunities, of which, if you're in this situation, you seize one and you're great over there instead of over here. There is an element of luck, yes and no. Luck favors a prepared mind; luck favors a prepared person. It is not guaranteed; I don't guarantee success as being absolutely certain. I'd say luck changes the odds, but there is some definite control on the part of the individual.
汉明：确实有运气的成分。但另一方面，我并不了解其他的发展路径。除非你能证明其他路径不会取得同样甚至更好的成果，否则我无法下定论。你所做的具体事情，算是运气吗？比如，我在洛斯阿拉莫斯遇到费曼时，就知道他会获得诺贝尔奖。我当时不知道他会因什么成就获奖，但我非常清楚他会做出伟大的研究。无论未来出现什么研究方向，这个人都会做出卓越的成果。事实也确实如此，他后来成就斐然。 并非只是在某个特定情境下做出一点伟大的工作就算是运气，人生中迟早会出现诸多机遇。就像满满一桶机遇，如果你处于合适的状态，抓住其中一个，就能在这边取得成就，而非在那边碌碌无为。这里面确实有运气的成分，但也不全是运气。机遇偏爱有准备的头脑，机遇偏爱有准备的人。成功并非必然，我也不敢保证成功是板上钉钉的事。我认为运气会改变成功的概率，但个人自身也能掌控一定的主动权。

Go forth, then, and do great work!
那么，前进吧，做伟大的工作！

(End of the General Research Colloquium Talk.)
（通用研究研讨会演讲结束。）

**BIOGRAPHICAL SKETCH OF RICHARD HAMMING
理查德·汉明传记**

理查德·W·哈明于 1915 年 2 月 11 日出生于伊利诺伊州芝加哥。他的正规教育包括以下学位（均为数学专业）：1937 年芝加哥大学学士学位；1939 年内布拉斯加大学硕士学位；以及 1942 年伊利诺伊大学博士学位。他的早期经历是在洛斯阿拉莫斯（1945-1946 年），即第二次世界大战结束时，他负责管理用于制造第一颗原子弹的计算机。从那里他直接去了贝尔实验室，在那里他在计算、数值分析和计算管理等各个方面工作了三十年，即 1946-1976 年。1976 年 7 月 23 日，他将“办公室”搬到了加利福尼亚州蒙特雷的海军研究生院，在那里他教书、指导研究并撰写书籍。

在贝尔实验室期间，他抽出时间在大学里任教，有时是本地授课，有时是全职休假；这些活动包括纽约大学的客座教授职位、普林斯顿大学（统计学）、纽约市立学院、斯坦福大学（1960-61 年）、史蒂文斯理工学院（数学）以及加州大学尔湾分校（1970-71 年）。

理查德·汉明获得了多项奖项，包括：1968 年成为 IEEE Fellow；1968 年获得 ACM 图灵奖；1979 年获得 IEEE 埃曼纽尔·R·皮奥雷奖；1980 年成为美国国家工程院院士；以及 1981 年获得宾夕法尼亚大学的哈罗德·彭德尔奖。1987 年，IEEE 设立了一项以他命名的重大奖项——理查德·W·汉明奖章，奖项名称为“为信息科学和系统做出杰出贡献”；恰如其分的是，他也是该奖项的第一位获得者，时间是 1988 年。1996 年在慕尼黑，他因在纠错码方面的研究工作，获得了价值 13 万美元的著名爱德华·莱因技术创新奖。他曾担任 ACM 的创始人和前任主席，以及美国科学促进会数学分部的副总统。

他最著名的成就可能是在纠错码、微分方程整合以及以他名字命名的频谱窗口方面的开创性工作。他的大量著作包括许多重要、开创性且备受推崇的书籍。这些书籍包括：
-   _Numerical Methods for Scientists and Engineers_, McGraw-Hill, 1962; Second edition 1973; Reprinted by Dover 1985; Translated into Russian.
-   _Calculus and the Computer Revolution_, Houghton-Mifflin, 1968.
-   _Introduction to Applied Numerical Analysis_, McGraw-Hill, 1971.
-   _Computers and Society_, McGraw-Hill, 1972.
-   _Digital Filters_, Prentice-Hall, 1977; Second edition 1983; Third edition 1989; translated into several European languages.
-   _Coding and Information Theory_, Prentice-Hall, 1980; Second edition 1986.
-   _Methods of Mathematics Applied to Calculus, Probability and Statistics_, Prentice-Hall, 1985.
-   _The Art of Probability for Scientists and Engineers_, Addison-Wesley, 1991.
-   _The Art of Doing Science and Engineering: Learning to Learn_, Gordon and Breach, 1997.

他继续以兼职教授的身份过着非常活跃的生活，在海军研究生院蒙蒂雷分校的数学与计算机科学系教学和写作，又过了二十一年才退休，并于 1997 年成为荣誉教授。他在 1997 年秋季仍在授课。他于 1998 年 1 月 7 日意外去世。
